# Stillness, Commerce, and What We Choose to Protect

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been sitting with something for a while now, and I am not entirely sure how to say it, but it feels important to share honestly rather than let it sit unspoken.

As I have been building Beyond the Bend Yoga, I keep noticing a particular tension. On one side, I genuinely want to create space for stillness and self-connection, for something real and unhurried. On the other side, I am running a business, which means there are websites and schedules and pricing pages and all the machinery that makes the work exist in the world. None of that is wrong. It is just the landscape, and I am learning to hold both at once. Some days it feels manageable. Other days it genuinely feels like walking a tightrope over something that matters.

What I keep bumping into is this question of what we choose to protect when something deeply personal meets the marketplace. Yoga did not begin as an industry. It began as a personal relationship with breath, sensation, and awareness, as a way of listening more carefully to yourself rather than looking outside for answers. And I notice how easily that center of gravity can shift. When a practice that is rooted in listening becomes something we consume, our attention starts to move outward. Am I doing this right? Is this what it is supposed to look like? Am I keeping up? Without meaning to, yoga can become another place where we strive and compare and shape ourselves, rather than somewhere we soften into what is actually happening.

I have felt this tension in my own body, not as a conflict exactly, but as a constant balancing act. The pull to offer more, to make things look a certain way, to fit into the language of productivity and progress that the world understands. And underneath that pull, the quieter voice that keeps asking why I started this in the first place. Yoga was never meant to make us more efficient or impressive. It was meant to help us become more present, to notice, to feel, to respond rather than react.

What feels most important to me right now is paying attention to how commercialization can subtly undermine inner authority. When people are taught, even unintentionally, to look outside themselves for validation or approval, they can slowly lose trust in their own experience. A practice meant to cultivate agency can, without care, do the opposite. I do not want that. I want the work I offer to be something that helps people trust themselves more, not less.

So this work, for me, is a continual act of remembering. Remembering why I began. Remembering what yoga has offered me in the moments it has been at its most honest. And remembering that stillness is not something I can package or promise. It is something I can protect space for, and that commitment shapes how I teach, how I structure what I offer, and how I walk through the business side of things. Not perfectly, not without friction, but with care.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
