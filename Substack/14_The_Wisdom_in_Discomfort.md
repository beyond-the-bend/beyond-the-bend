# The Wisdom in Discomfort

*By Laura Harvey*

I want to share something I have been learning about the relationship between discomfort and clarity, something that has shifted how I understand purpose, presence, and what the body is actually asking for when we feel unsettled.

There is a pattern I see often in myself and in the women I work with: the impulse to move quickly when discomfort arises. To decide. To take action. To make the unsettled feeling go away so we can feel safe again. I know this pattern well because my nervous system learned it early. Staying still inside discomfort did not feel safe. So I became good at moving, deciding, fixing, pushing forward. Even when things looked calm on the outside, there was often urgency underneath, always leaning toward resolution.

What I have come to understand is that the urgency was not intuition. It was habit. It was a nervous system that had learned to associate relief with motion, and stillness with danger. The difference matters enormously, because when we act from that habit, we often move before we have actually heard what the discomfort was trying to tell us.

When we allow ourselves to feel discomfort without immediately reacting to it, something surprising happens. The intensity softens, not because we made it soften, but because it was allowed to complete its own cycle. We start to notice that discomfort has texture and shape. It rises. It peaks. It settles. And often, underneath it, there is something much quieter waiting to be felt, something that had been buried under the busyness of our response to it.

This has changed how I understand purpose. I used to believe purpose would feel motivating and energizing, like a strong yes in the body that pushes us forward. And sometimes it does feel like that. But more often, purpose feels subtle. Almost shy. It shows up when we stop demanding certainty and start listening instead. It does not arrive as confidence or a dramatic sense of direction. It arrives as a kind of gentle steadiness, a feeling of being rooted even when the mind is unsure, a sense of rightness that does not need to be defended or explained.

Discomfort, it turns out, is often the doorway, not because it is pleasant, but because it slows us down enough to notice what is actually true. When we stop trying to escape it, we can hear ourselves more clearly, not the voice that panics or rushes, but the quieter one that has been present all along, waiting for us to stop moving long enough to listen.

I do not feel like I am on the other side of this yet. I am very much in it. But what I am beginning to sense is that staying with discomfort is not a failure or a delay. It might actually be part of the process of becoming more honest with myself. If purpose feels fuzzy or distant right now, it may not be something to chase. It may be something that meets us when we are finally willing to stay exactly where we are.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
