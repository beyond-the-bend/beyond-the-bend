# Softening into Surrender

*By Laura Harvey*

Christmas is four days away and I have been staring at the same half-finished cup of tea for twenty minutes, not because I am sad exactly, but because something has been asking for my attention all week. It is something quiet, underneath all the noise of the season, and I keep almost hearing it and then losing it again in the busyness of everything around me. I finally sat down this morning and decided to actually listen.

The word that keeps surfacing is surrender, and I have been sitting with that because I realize I have been using it in my classes for years without fully understanding what it was asking of me personally. I have talked about it with students, written it into sequences, invited it in as a concept. But saying a word and living inside it turn out to be very different things, and the last few months have been making that gap quite clear.

Here is what I used to think surrender meant: giving up, stepping back, a kind of graceful defeat wrapped in spiritual language. I associated it with something passive, even a little tragic, as though it were what you chose when you had run out of better options. That is not what I have found it to be.

For me, surrender tends to arrive when pushing stops working. It does not come as a decision so much as something that happens in the body before the mind has caught up. My shoulders drop further than I told them to. My breath finds its way lower without any instruction from me. My jaw lets go. And somewhere in that, I notice that I have been holding something, though I do not always know exactly what it was. There is always this moment of recognizing how much energy something was costing me that I did not even know I was spending.

I think midlife does something specific to this. There is a particular tiredness that builds over decades of holding things together, staying functional, staying composed, making things work without complaint. It is not the kind of tired you fix with a good night's sleep because it sits deeper than that, in the tissue of the effort itself. Surrender tends to arrive right there, not as a dramatic moment, but as a quiet and honest recognition that I cannot keep meeting life in this particular way.

What continues to surprise me is what comes after that moment of softening. When I stop bracing, even slightly, there is more clarity than I expected. Decisions that felt tangled start to feel simpler, not because they have become easy, but because I can hear myself again. I listen differently. I notice things I had been walking right past: small moments of beauty, the sound of the house in the morning, the particular quality of winter light through the kitchen window. None of that is dramatic, but it changes something about how it actually feels to be inside your own life.

A student mentioned after class last week that she had finally allowed herself to cry during the practice. She said she had been holding that for months and had not realized it until it moved through her. She looked lighter afterward, not fixed, but softer. I recognized that look because I have worn it myself, and I said to her what I keep coming back to for myself: you cannot force the release. You can only make room for it. The practice creates the conditions by slowing the breath and letting the nervous system understand that it is safe, and then the body begins to release what it has been carrying in its own time.

If surrender feels far away right now, or if it sounds soft or a little naive in the middle of everything this season is asking of you, I understand that. I do not think it needs to arrive as a big decision or a turning point. Sometimes it is much more ordinary than that, like allowing yourself to rest without needing to explain it to yourself first, or quietly admitting that something no longer fits. That is surrender too, and it counts just as much as the bigger moments. I am going to finish this tea now, and then I am going to sit for a few minutes with my hands in my lap and let my nervous system remember that it does not have to hold everything quite so tight. That is the whole practice this week.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
