# The Space Between Thoughts

*By Laura Harvey*

Have you ever noticed how loud the mind can become the very moment you decide you want quiet? It is almost comical. You sit down, you take a breath, you set an intention toward stillness, and immediately the brain offers you a grocery list, an old conversation you should have handled differently, a worry about something three weeks from now, and completely unnecessary commentary about something you said years ago that you had mostly forgotten.

For so many of us, peace sounds beautiful in theory, right up until we sit down to actually feel it. Then the inner chatter begins, and we assume something must be wrong with us. But there is not. What is happening is perfectly ordinary. The mind has a very strong habit of filling every quiet pause with noise, because somewhere along the way it learned that staying busy felt safer than being still. It rehearses, replays, evaluates, judges, and tries to predict what might go wrong, not because we are broken, but because the nervous system is doing exactly what it has always done to keep us oriented and ready. The problem is that this mechanism was designed for genuine uncertainty and danger, not for a quiet Tuesday morning when we are trying to breathe.

Most of us were never taught how to relate to our thoughts, only how to follow them. We spend years believing every storyline that arises, assuming that if the mind presents something urgently enough, it must be true. The invitation of any contemplative practice, whether that is yoga or meditation or simply pausing before you respond to something, is not to silence the mind or make the thoughts stop. It is to change our relationship to them. When we start to recognize mental activity rather than being entirely inside it, something subtle and genuinely remarkable happens. We create just the smallest bit of space. And in that space, we reconnect with something older and steadier than any of our thoughts, the quiet awareness underneath them that does not change when the thoughts do.

Imagine a stream winding gently through a forest. Thoughts are like leaves drifting across the surface: some bright and useful, some heavy and tangled, all of them moving through. The habit is to grab at them, to chase the beautiful ones and resist the uncomfortable ones and try to make sense of every single one. But the stream keeps moving regardless. And when we practice letting them pass rather than reaching in, something remarkable happens. The water becomes clearer. Not because we made it clear, but because we finally stopped disturbing it.

We are not our thoughts. We are the awareness that witnesses them. And when we can rest even briefly in that awareness rather than in the content of whoever is thinking loudest at the moment, something feels different. Not dramatic or performative, just a quiet rightness, like remembering something we always knew but had been too busy to notice. The peace does not require perfect conditions. It is revealed when the noise settles, even for a moment, even for a breath.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
