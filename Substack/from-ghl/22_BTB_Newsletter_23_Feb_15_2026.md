## This Week's Wellness Wisdom


### The Muscle of the Soul


*By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga*


There is a deep muscle in the center of your body called the **psoas** (pronounced so-as).
While anatomy books will tell you it helps you lift your leg, it is actually much more special than
that.


### The Anatomy of Connection


It is the *only* muscle in the human body that connects our upper body (the spine) to our lower
body (the legs). It literally bridges the gap between our breathing and our walking.


It starts deep in your lower back, flows through your pelvis, and attaches to the top of your inner
thigh. This means every step you take, and every breath you take, ripples through this deep core muscle.


Those who work deeply with the body, like the wonderful somatic educator **Liz Koch**, call
it something else. She calls it the **“muscle of the soul.”**


I love this description because it honors what many of us have felt but haven't had the words for.


Think of your psoas as the body’s emotional drawer. It is deeply connected to our nervous system, our
"fight or flight" response. When the world feels heavy, or when the "noise" of life gets too loud, this
deep muscle tightens up to protect you. It curls you in, holding onto the stress so you can keep going,
keep doing, keep holding it all together for everyone else.


It does such a good job that we often forget it’s even holding on. Until we stop.


When we come to our practice, we aren't trying to "stretch" this muscle into submission. As Liz Koch
teaches, we can’t force the psoas to let go. We have to make it feel safe enough to soften.


This is why, in our classes, we focus so much on just *being* rather than *doing*. When we
force a stretch, the psoas often tightens up even more to protect us. But when we offer it support, like
the floor, a bolster, or a quiet moment, we send a signal to that deep, primal part of ourselves that
says, *"You are safe now. You can put the burden down."*


Sometimes, when the psoas finally softens, those stored feelings bubble up. We call this a somatic
release. It isn't a sign that something is wrong. It is a sign that something is finally moving. It’s
just the body’s way of exhaling what it no longer needs to carry.


This release is rarely logical. You might be thinking about your grocery list, yet your body is
processing a conversation from three years ago. That is the wisdom of the psoas. It holds the history we
have forgotten. By giving it permission to soften, we aren't just stretching a hip flexor. We are
tending to the parts of ourselves that have been standing guard for far too long.


## OUR WEEKLY PRACTICE:


### Unwinding the Core


This is not a checklist or something to get right. It is simply an invitation you can return to when
you need to feel held.


- **Find Constructive Rest:** Instead of trying to stretch your hips, simply lie on
your back with your knees bent and your feet flat on the floor, about hip-width apart. Let your
hands rest on your belly.


- **Do Nothing:** This is the hardest part. Don't try to flatten your back or
"engage" your core. Just let gravity hold your thigh bones. Let the ground hold your spine.


- **Notice the Holding:** Gently scan your body. Are you gripping in your jaw? Your
glutes? Your shoulders? Don’t try to fix it, just notice it. Acknowledge that your body has been
working hard to protect you.


- **The Micro-Soothe:** If being perfectly still feels too loud, try a tiny movement.
Gently rock your knees side to side, less than an inch. This rhythmic, watery movement reminds
your nervous system that you are fluid, not frozen.


- **Offer Safety:** On an exhale, imagine the deep muscles in your pelvis melting
like butter in a warm pan. If emotion arises, let it. You don't need to analyze it or know where
it came from. Just let it move through.


- **Stay for 10 Breaths:** Give yourself permission to stay here until you feel a
genuine exhale, the kind that drops your shoulders away from your ears.


## A Closing Thought


If you find yourself feeling a little tender or teary during a practice, or even just in the quiet
moments of your day, please be gentle with yourself. We spend so much energy holding it all together. It
is a brave and beautiful thing to finally let yourself fall apart, even just a little bit, into the
safety of your own breath.


It’s just another form of movement. It’s the sound of your body finally coming home to itself.
