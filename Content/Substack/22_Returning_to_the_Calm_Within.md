# Returning to the Calm Within

*By Laura Harvey*

September has arrived with its own particular buzz and I have been noticing it in my body before I even noticed it in the calendar. My shoulders moved a little higher. My breath got a little shallower. The to-do list started multiplying in my mind before the week had even properly begun, full of things to organize, tasks to complete, details that felt suddenly urgent even though most of them had been sitting quietly for weeks without any urgency at all.

I caught myself rushing through things that did not actually need to be rushed, simply because the world felt fast and I had unconsciously decided to match it. That is the thing about September, it carries the ghost of back-to-school even for those of us who have not been in school in decades. There is something in the air that whispers: now, get ready, do not fall behind. And without even choosing it, we shift into a mode that has more to do with an old learned rhythm than with what is actually required of us right now.

I have been returning this week to something I first came across years ago called the Three Principles, a framework for understanding human experience developed by Michael Neill and others. The core insight is deceptively simple: we do not experience the world as it is. We experience it through our own thinking, moment by moment. That is why two people can move through the same situation and feel entirely different things. The event itself is the same. What each person makes of it, the thoughts they bring to it, is not.

This is a reminder I find genuinely comforting. Because it means the buzz I feel in September is not really coming from my calendar or my inbox or the length of the to-do list. It is being generated in my own mind, and like all generated weather, it will shift and clear on its own if I let it. The thoughts are real. The feelings that come from them are real. But they are not permanent, and they are not the only truth available to me in this moment.

What I keep coming back to is that peace is not something I need to earn when the list is finished. It is available right here, in the middle of it all, if I can remember to look for it. A pause, a breath, a single moment of actually noticing where I am rather than where I am trying to get to. That is enough to change something. Not everything, not immediately, but enough. The mind quiets just slightly in those moments, and in that quiet, what truly matters tends to have more room to rise.

My invitation for this week, and for myself too, is this: notice where you are choosing busyness over peace, and gently ask whether the urgency is actually serving you. You are allowed to move through this season at your own pace. The calm that feels out of reach is much closer than you think.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
