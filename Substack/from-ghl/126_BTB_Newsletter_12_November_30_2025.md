## Hello

I’ve been noticing a specific energy in the air this week. Can you feel it?

It is a hum of urgency.

Everywhere we look right now, from our inboxes to the grocery store lineups, the message seems to be the same. Hurry. Don’t miss out. Act now. Move faster.

I found myself catching that energy yesterday. My shoulders crept up toward my ears and I rushed through tasks that didn't actually need to be rushed, just because the world felt fast.

It made me pause and wonder why we feel we have to match the speed of the chaos around us.

There is something radical about choosing to move slowly when everything else is spinning. It feels like a small rebellion. It is a way of reclaiming your own peace and reminding your body that, despite the noise, you are safe here.

I hope this week’s reflection offers you a little permission to unclench, exhale, and take your time.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura 🩷

September arrives with its own kind of hum. For many of us, it stirs up the old rhythm of back-to-school, fresh starts, new schedules — and with it, a rush of shoulds and self-imposed pressure.

Suddenly our minds are full of lists: things to organize, tasks to complete, details that must get handled. And if we don’t tick every box, the inner critic shows up: “Why can’t I keep up?”

But here’s the thing: stress doesn’t make the work go faster. Busyness doesn’t always mean productivity. Often, it’s just noise.

What if September could feel different this year?

What if, instead of being pulled into the whirlwind, you brought a little spaciousness into your days? A pause between the tasks. A few deep breaths before the next thing. A moment to remember: peace isn’t earned after the list is done — it’s available right here, in the middle of it all.

Because when the mind is calmer, the noise fades. And when the noise fades, what truly matters has room to rise.

## **The Habit of Running**

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

The teacher Thich Nhat Hanh often spoke about something he called "The Habit of Running."

He used the Sanskrit word Vāsanā to describe this. It translates to "habit energy," which is the deep-seated momentum of the past pushing us around in the present.

He observed that so many of us spend our entire lives running. We do it internally, even when we are sitting still. We run while we are eating. We run even in our sleep.

We are always leaning forward into the next moment because this momentum is so strong. It convinces us that if we just hurry fast enough, we will finally "catch up." We think once we catch up, then we can relax.

But deep down, we know the truth. The finish line keeps moving. There is always another list, another request, another role to fill.

Living in this state of constant running doesn't actually make us more efficient. It just makes us empty. It steals the texture of our lives. When we rush through making dinner, we miss the smell of the spices. When we rush through a conversation, we miss the connection.

We end up skimming the surface of our own lives.

Thich Nhat Hanh taught that as long as we are running, we are not capable of touching the peace of the present moment. We miss the sunshine, the fresh air, and the friend sitting right in front of us.

The truth is, we will never truly be "caught up." And that is good news. It means we don't have to wait for an empty to-do list to find calm. We can choose to be peaceful right now, even with things left undone.

Slowing down isn’t about falling behind. It is about deciding to inhabit the moment you are actually in.

When we begin to see this truth clearly, it changes how we approach love — not as something to find, earn, or chase, but as something that begins within. The way we treat ourselves becomes the soil from which every other relationship grows.

Imagine, for a moment, our inner world as a garden. Every thought, every act of care, every boundary and breath becomes part of its landscape. Some days it may look lush and alive — new growth reaching toward the sun. Other days, it may feel untended — overgrown with old habits or dry from neglect.

This garden isn’t a place to perfect. It’s a place to notice.
To be curious about what’s growing.
To acknowledge what’s struggling.
To gently tend what needs attention.

When we nurture ourselves with gentleness and honesty, that energy naturally extends outward. It softens our tone, steadies our presence, and deepens our capacity to love others without losing ourselves. But when we neglect our inner garden — through self-criticism, avoidance, or over-giving — the soil becomes depleted. Even when love comes near, it can’t quite take root.

Many of us learned long ago to earn love through doing, pleasing, or achieving. Those patterns were intelligent; they kept us connected in the ways we knew how. But as we grow older, they begin to feel heavy — as if the garden we’ve been tending was built to please everyone but ourselves.

Healing begins the moment we bring awareness to this cycle — when we pause, take a breath, and ask, What would it feel like to meet ourselves with curiosity instead of judgment?

That single question is like sunlight breaking through clouds. It doesn’t demand that we fix or change — only that we notice, and allow. Each act of awareness becomes a drop of water for the roots of self-trust. Each kind thought becomes nourishment for the soil of our hearts.

Over time, the garden shifts. We begin to see new shoots of growth — boundaries formed from clarity rather than fear, choices made from love rather than guilt, tenderness that doesn’t depend on anyone else’s approval.

We don’t need to be “fixed” or endlessly giving to be worthy of love. We only need to meet ourselves where we are — with patience, tenderness, and care.

Because when we do, our roots grow stronger. And from that fertile ground, every connection in our lives — with others, with the world, and with our own hearts — begins to bloom in a way that feels more peaceful, more authentic, and more true.

## **Practice of the Week: **


## **The Half-Speed Task**

In the spirit of stopping the run, I invite you to try a very small experiment this week.

Choose one ordinary, mundane task that you usually rush through. Maybe it’s making your morning coffee, folding the laundry, or walking from your car to the front door.

And then, just for those few minutes, **do it at half-speed.**

- Notice the weight of the kettle in your hand.


- Feel the texture of the fabric as you fold it.


- Feel your heel, then your arch, then your toe strike the ground as you walk.


- Take a full breath between each movement.


Notice how your mind might want to pull you forward ("Come on, let's get this done!").

When you feel that pull, simply pause. Take a slow inhale through your nose and let it out with a soft sigh.

Remind yourself that there is nowhere else you need to be in this exact second. Feel your shoulders drop away from your ears.

That tiny pocket of slowness is where the magic lives.
