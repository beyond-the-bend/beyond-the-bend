The Habit of Running

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

The teacher Thich Nhat Hanh often spoke about something he called
"The Habit of Running."

He used the Sanskrit word Vāsanā to describe this. It translates to
"habit energy," which is the deep-seated momentum of the past pushing
us around in the present.

He observed that so many of us spend our entire lives running. We do it
internally, even when we are sitting still. We run while we are eating. We
run even in our sleep.

We are always leaning forward into the next moment because this
momentum is so strong. It convinces us that if we just hurry fast
enough, we will finally "catch up." We think once we catch up, then we
can relax.

But deep down, we know the truth. The finish line keeps moving. There
is always another list, another request, another role to fill.
Living in this state of constant running doesn't actually make us more
efficient. It just makes us empty. It steals the texture of our lives. When
we rush through making dinner, we miss the smell of the spices. When
we rush through a conversation, we miss the connection.

We end up skimming the surface of our own lives.

Thich Nhat Hanh taught that as long as we are running, we are not
capable of touching the peace of the present moment. We miss the
sunshine, the fresh air, and the friend sitting right in front of us.

The truth is, we will never truly be "caught up." And that is good news. It
means we don't have to wait for an empty to-do list to find calm. We can
choose to be peaceful right now, even with things left undone.

Slowing down isn’t about falling behind. It is about deciding to inhabit
the moment you are actually in.
Practice of the Week:

The Half-Speed Task

In the spirit of stopping the run, I invite you to try a very small
experiment this week.

Choose one ordinary, mundane task that you usually rush through.
Maybe it’s making your morning coffee, folding the laundry, or walking
from your car to the front door.

And then, just for those few minutes, do it at half-speed.

      Notice the weight of the kettle in your hand.

      Feel the texture of the fabric as you fold it.

      Feel your heel, then your arch, then your toe strike the ground as
      you walk.

      Take a full breath between each movement.

Notice how your mind might want to pull you forward ("Come on, let's
get this done!").

When you feel that pull, simply pause. Take a slow inhale through your
nose and let it out with a soft sigh.

Remind yourself that there is nowhere else you need to be in this exact
second. Feel your shoulders drop away from your ears.

That tiny pocket of slowness is where the magic lives.
