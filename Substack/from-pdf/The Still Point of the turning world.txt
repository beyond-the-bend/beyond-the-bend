The Turning World, The Still Point, and You

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

The Wheel

I notice this tension in my own life. It feels like living on the edge of a
giant, spinning wheel. There's the to do list, the caretaking, the business
of living, the schedules, the noise. That outer edge (let's call it The Rim)
spins incredibly fast.

And I notice that when I live entirely on the Rim, I get dizzy. I feel
scattered. I catch myself thinking that if I just spin a little faster, or
manage the systems a little better, I'll finally catch up and find some
peace.

But the spinning never really stops, does it?

Here's the thing I've been grappling with: None of that is wrong. The
wheel has to turn. The laundry needs doing; the work needs tending.
We can't just step off the world.

But if you look at a wheel, there's another part. The Hub. The Hub is the
axle in the center. It carries all the weight. It allows the movement to
happen. But the Hub itself? It doesn't spin.
Moving Your Seat

I'm realizing that my practice (and what I hope to offer you) isn't about
trying to stop the wheel of your life. It's simply about moving your seat.

It's about shifting your attention from the exhausted, dizzying Rim back
toward the Hub.

When we practice together, we aren't trying to "fix" the chaos or become
more efficient at spinning. We're simply remembering that there's a
place inside you (your breath, your spine, your deep "self") that is the
Still Point.

It's the place that moves, but does not spin.

This isn't a one time arrival. It's a practice of returning. Again and again.
Some days we find ourselves flung back out to the Rim, caught up in
urgency, overwhelm, or the hundred small emergencies of daily life. And
that's okay. The Hub is always there, waiting.

The body knows how to find it. The breath knows the way back.

What This Looks Like in Practice

For some women, the Still Point shows up in a long held restorative
pose, where the nervous system finally exhales and says, "Oh. I'm
safe."

For others, it's the moment in meditation when the mental chatter
softens and you realize you've been watching your thoughts instead of
becoming them.

And sometimes, it's as simple as noticing your feet on the ground and
remembering: I am here. I am whole. I don't have to spin.

I don't have tidy answers on how to stay there permanently. The world is
loud, and the pull of the Rim is strong. But I do know that when we
soften the belly and find our breath, we protect a little space for that
stillness to land.

We practice not to be perfect at stillness, but to become fluent in the
language of return.

Practice of the Week
Finding the Axis

This week, I'm inviting you to try a very small practice. You don't need a
yoga mat for this.

When you feel that familiar "rush" or tightness in your chest (when the
emails are piling up or the noise is getting loud) try this:

   1. Stop moving for just ten seconds.

   2. Feel your seat in the chair or your feet on the floor.

   3. Imagine your spine is the Hub of the wheel.

   4. Ask yourself: "Am I spinning right now? Or am I centered?"

You don't have to fix it. Just noticing that you're on the Rim is often
enough to bring you back toward the center.

If it helps, you might even place one hand on your heart and whisper: "I
can return to the Hub. The Still Point is always here."

That's it. That's the whole practice.
