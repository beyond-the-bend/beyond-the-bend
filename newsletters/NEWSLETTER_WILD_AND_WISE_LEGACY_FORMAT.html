This Week's Wellness Wisdom

The Wild and The Wise

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

In simple terms, the energy of the Horse is about movement, vitality, and a certain kind of unapologetic aliveness. Not the frantic, breathless doing we are so often called into, the kind that leaves us depleted and wondering what we were even running toward. This is something different. It is a natural, rhythmic power. A dignity in motion.

We often think of wildness as something belonging only to youth. We associate it with being reckless, or unformed, or impractical. Something to be eventually tamed.

But there is a different kind of wildness that arrives later in life. It doesn't announce itself loudly. It arrives more like a quiet recognition, a slow clearing of the throat. It is the wildness of a woman who has stopped asking for permission to be herself.

I think of it as the wise wild.

It is what happens when the years of caretaking, of shape-shifting to fit other people's needs, of making ourselves smaller so others could feel larger, when all of that begins to loosen its grip. Not with bitterness. Not with resentment. But with a kind of gentle, almost amused clarity.

Oh. So this is what was here all along.

For so many of us in midlife, there comes a moment, or a slow season of moments, when we realize that our energy is finally becoming our own again. We are no longer the horse pulling the carriage for everyone else, keeping the pace set by someone else's schedule, someone else's needs, someone else's vision of who we should be.

We are the horse discovering the open field.

And that field is not about doing more. It is not a new to-do list disguised as freedom. It is something quieter and more fundamental: the reclaiming of your own rhythm. The permission to move at the pace that is actually yours. To feel strong and unhurried at the same time.

That, I think, is what this season is inviting.

OUR WEEKLY PRACTICE:

Rooting to Rise

You don't need a mat for this. You don't need a quiet room or the perfect moment. You just need a minute, and wherever you already are.

  • Connect with the Ground: Take a moment to sit or stand tall, feeling the soles of your feet connecting with whatever is beneath you (floor, earth, carpet, wood). It doesn't matter. It is still ground.
  • Anchor Your Weight: Notice where you feel the most weight in your body. Let that weight be what it is — not a problem to fix, but an anchor. A point of contact with something solid.
  • Rise with Inhale: On your next inhale, imagine a steady, horse-like energy rising from the earth through the soles of your feet, moving slowly upward into your belly.
  • Quiet Release: Let your exhale be a quiet release, not a dramatic letting go, just a gentle unclenching of whatever you have been carrying for others today.
  • Linger: Linger here for three breaths. Not trying to feel anything in particular. Just noticing what it is like to be both grounded and alive at the same time.

That is enough. That is, in fact, quite a lot.

A Closing Thought

I keep coming back to that image: the horse discovering the open field. Not running wildly, not standing still, but simply moving as itself. No longer in service of someone else's destination.

I think that is the invitation of this season. Not to reinvent yourself. Not to add more. But to remember what your own rhythm actually feels like, and to trust it.
