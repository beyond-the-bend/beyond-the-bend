This Week's Wellness Wisdom

Swimming With the River

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

Watts writes about the Tao, the fundamental flow of everything, and he compares it to a tremendous river that carries us all along whether we know it or not. Some people swim against the river, he says, and yet they are still being carried. Others have learned to swim with it. They are carried along too, but they are aware of it.

The ones swimming against the current think they are going in the opposite direction. But they're not. Not really.

And then he says this, which is the part I wrote "great analogy" beside in the margin:

The person who gives up that pretense, who realizes they are inevitably carried by the river and chooses to swim with it, suddenly acquires behind everything they do the force of the river itself.

I think about who I was in my twenties, and I recognize someone who was swimming hard against the current and calling it ambition.

I thought life was something I had to make happen through sheer force of will. I gripped everything so tightly. My plans, my relationships, my ideas about how things were supposed to go. If something wasn't working, I pushed harder. And when that still didn't produce the result I wanted, I blamed myself for not trying hard enough.

That is an exhausting way to live. And I lived that way for a very long time.

What I didn't understand then is that the river was carrying me the whole time. Even when I was thrashing against it. Even when I was convinced I was failing. The current was still moving. Life was still unfolding.

Most of the suffering I felt came not from what was actually happening, but from my insistence that it should be happening differently.

I'm in my fifties now, and something has shifted that I don't think I could have rushed even if I'd wanted to. I've stopped trying to force the river to go where I think it should.

Not because I've given up. Not because I no longer care where I'm headed. It's actually the opposite. I care more deeply than I ever have. But I've learned that the caring doesn't require the gripping. I can have a direction without needing to control every turn. I can move with intention and still allow life to carry me where it needs to.

This is what surrender actually looks like. And it's nothing like what I used to think it was.

I used to believe surrender meant quitting. Lying down. Letting the world walk over you. But real surrender is the moment you stop exhausting yourself fighting what is already in motion, and you let the force of the river move through what you do instead.

It's not weakness. It's the most powerful thing I've ever experienced.

In my yoga practice and in my teaching, I see this constantly. The students who grip and force their way into a pose are the ones who struggle the most. The ones who breathe into it, who allow the body to open rather than demanding that it comply, are the ones who find the depth they were looking for.

It's the same principle. Just lived through the body instead of through philosophy.

The river is always there. In our breath. In our movement. In the quiet moments between all the doing.

Practice of the Week

Where Are You Swimming Against the River?

This week, I want to invite you into a simple noticing practice. You don't need a mat or a timer. You just need a few honest minutes with yourself.

At some point today, or tomorrow, or whenever it finds you, pause and ask yourself:

Where in my life am I forcing right now?

It might be a relationship you're trying to control. A situation at work you keep pushing against. A plan that isn't unfolding the way you decided it should. Or even something as small as rushing through your morning when your body is asking you to slow down.

Once you've found it, try this:

Take a breath. A real one. Let your shoulders drop. And just for a moment, stop swimming.

You don't have to fix anything. You don't have to let go of what matters to you. Just notice what it feels like to stop fighting the current, even for thirty seconds.

Ask yourself: What would it feel like to let the river carry me here, instead of exhausting myself trying to redirect it?

You might feel relief. You might feel fear. You might feel nothing at all. Whatever comes is fine.

Try this once a day for the rest of the week. Just the pause. Just the question. And notice if anything starts to soften.

A Closing Thought

If you've spent years swimming against the current, I want you to know something. The exhaustion you feel is not a sign that you aren't trying hard enough. It might be a sign that you've been trying too hard at the wrong thing.

The river was carrying you all along. It still is.
