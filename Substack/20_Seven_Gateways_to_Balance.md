# Seven Gateways to Balance

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been re-exploring a book called Eastern Body, Western Mind recently, and it has been doing something unexpected for me. It takes the ancient chakra system, the seven energy centers mapped in the body within yogic tradition, and pairs it with modern psychology to explore how our life experiences shape us from the inside out. I expected it to feel somewhat academic. Instead it has been feeling uncomfortably personal, in the good way.

The chakra system is not something I have always known how to talk about in a contemporary context, because a lot of the language around it can feel either too esoteric or too simplified. But what Anodea Judith does in this book is ground it in something I recognize from my own life and from the stories of women I work with. Each center reflects a layer of experience, from the most primal, do I feel safe, do I have what I need to survive, do I belong here, all the way through to the most expansive, do I feel connected to something larger than my individual life. And when you look at it that way, it stops being abstract very quickly.

In midlife especially, questions like these tend to surface with a particular urgency. Not because midlife is a crisis, though it can feel that way for stretches, but because this is often the first time many of us have had enough space, enough distance from survival mode, to actually ask them. The root of who we are. Whether we trust our own instincts. Whether we feel allowed to take up space or speak our truth. Whether our hearts are open the way we want them to be.

What I find most useful about this framework is that it is not about climbing to some elevated state. It is about noticing where you actually are, where there is flow and where there is something stuck or depleted or still asking for attention. The root chakra speaks to safety and belonging. The sacral to pleasure, creativity, and the freedom to feel. The solar plexus to personal power and the right to act on your own behalf. The heart to love and connection. The throat to honest self-expression. The third eye to intuition and inner vision. The crown to a sense of meaning and of being held by something beyond the personal.

Most of us have some of these flowing well and others that have been in service to the ones that weren't, compensating, overworking, shutting down. That is worth knowing. Not to create a project out of fixing yourself, but simply to bring curiosity to where you are and what might be wanting attention.

That is the invitation this week. Not to climb anywhere new, but to pause and notice where you feel most alive, and where something might be quietly waiting for a little more care.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
