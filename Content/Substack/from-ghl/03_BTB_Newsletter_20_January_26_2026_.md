Because when the mind is calmer, the noise fades. And when the noise fades, what truly matters has room to rise.

## **The Turning World, The Still Point, and You**

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga


**The Wheel**

I notice this tension in my own life. It feels like living on the edge of a giant, spinning wheel. There's the to do list, the caretaking, the business of living, the schedules, the noise. That outer edge (let's call it **The Rim**) spins incredibly fast.

And I notice that when I live entirely on the Rim, I get dizzy. I feel scattered. I catch myself thinking that if I just spin a little faster, or manage the systems a little better, I'll finally catch up and find some peace.

But the spinning never really stops, does it?

Here's the thing I've been grappling with: None of that is *wrong*. The wheel has to turn. The laundry needs doing; the work needs tending. We can't just step off the world.

But if you look at a wheel, there's another part. **The Hub**. The Hub is the axle in the center. It carries all the weight. It allows the movement to happen. But the Hub itself? It doesn't spin.

**Moving Your Seat**

I'm realizing that my practice (and what I hope to offer you) isn't about trying to stop the wheel of your life. It's simply about **moving your seat**.

It's about shifting your attention from the exhausted, dizzying Rim back toward the Hub.

When we practice together, we aren't trying to "fix" the chaos or become more efficient at spinning. We're simply remembering that there's a place inside you (your breath, your spine, your deep "self") that *is* the Still Point.

It's the place that moves, but does not spin.

This isn't a one time arrival. It's a practice of returning. Again and again. Some days we find ourselves flung back out to the Rim, caught up in urgency, overwhelm, or the hundred small emergencies of daily life. And that's okay. The Hub is always there, waiting.

The body knows how to find it. The breath knows the way back.

**What This Looks Like in Practice**

For some women, the Still Point shows up in a long held restorative pose, where the nervous system finally exhales and says, *"Oh. I'm safe."*

For others, it's the moment in meditation when the mental chatter softens and you realize you've been watching your thoughts instead of becoming them.

And sometimes, it's as simple as noticing your feet on the ground and remembering: *I am here. I am whole. I don't have to spin.*

I don't have tidy answers on how to stay there permanently. The world is loud, and the pull of the Rim is strong. But I do know that when we soften the belly and find our breath, we protect a little space for that stillness to land.

We practice not to be perfect at stillness, but to become fluent in the language of return.

When we begin to see this truth clearly, it changes how we approach love — not as something to find, earn, or chase, but as something that begins within. The way we treat ourselves becomes the soil from which every other relationship grows.

Imagine, for a moment, our inner world as a garden. Every thought, every act of care, every boundary and breath becomes part of its landscape. Some days it may look lush and alive — new growth reaching toward the sun. Other days, it may feel untended — overgrown with old habits or dry from neglect.

This garden isn’t a place to perfect. It’s a place to notice.
To be curious about what’s growing.
To acknowledge what’s struggling.
To gently tend what needs attention.

When we nurture ourselves with gentleness and honesty, that energy naturally extends outward. It softens our tone, steadies our presence, and deepens our capacity to love others without losing ourselves. But when we neglect our inner garden — through self-criticism, avoidance, or over-giving — the soil becomes depleted. Even when love comes near, it can’t quite take root.

Many of us learned long ago to earn love through doing, pleasing, or achieving. Those patterns were intelligent; they kept us connected in the ways we knew how. But as we grow older, they begin to feel heavy — as if the garden we’ve been tending was built to please everyone but ourselves.

Healing begins the moment we bring awareness to this cycle — when we pause, take a breath, and ask, What would it feel like to meet ourselves with curiosity instead of judgment?

That single question is like sunlight breaking through clouds. It doesn’t demand that we fix or change — only that we notice, and allow. Each act of awareness becomes a drop of water for the roots of self-trust. Each kind thought becomes nourishment for the soil of our hearts.

Over time, the garden shifts. We begin to see new shoots of growth — boundaries formed from clarity rather than fear, choices made from love rather than guilt, tenderness that doesn’t depend on anyone else’s approval.

We don’t need to be “fixed” or endlessly giving to be worthy of love. We only need to meet ourselves where we are — with patience, tenderness, and care.

Because when we do, our roots grow stronger. And from that fertile ground, every connection in our lives — with others, with the world, and with our own hearts — begins to bloom in a way that feels more peaceful, more authentic, and more true.

## Practice of the Week

**Finding the Axis**

This week, I'm inviting you to try a very small practice. You don't need a yoga mat for this.

When you feel that familiar "rush" or tightness in your chest (when the emails are piling up or the noise is getting loud) try this:

- Stop moving for just ten seconds.


- Feel your seat in the chair or your feet on the floor.


- Imagine your spine is the Hub of the wheel.


- Ask yourself: "*Am I spinning right now? Or am I centered?*"


You don't have to fix it. Just noticing that you're on the Rim is often enough to bring you back toward the center.

If it helps, you might even place one hand on your heart and whisper: *"I can return to the Hub. The Still Point is always here."*

That's it. That's the whole practice.
