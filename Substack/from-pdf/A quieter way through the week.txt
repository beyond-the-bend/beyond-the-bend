Softening into Surrender

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

I have been thinking a lot about surrender lately, not as a theory, but as
a very lived and sometimes uncomfortable concept. It has always been
part of my teaching and my practice, yet only in the last few years have I
begun to understand more fully what it asks of me and what it quietly
gives back in return.

In my earlier years, I thought surrender meant letting things fall apart or
stepping away from responsibility, but I see now that it is neither of
those things. For me, surrender tends to appear when pushing stops
working, when effort no longer brings clarity, and when trying to manage
everything quietly becomes exhausting in a way that can no longer be
ignored.

There is a particular tiredness that I believe often comes with midlife,
and it is not the kind that a nap or a good night’s sleep can fix. It is
deeper than that, and it comes from years of holding things together,
making things work, staying functional, and remaining composed even
when things feel heavy. Surrender often enters right there, not as a
dramatic moment, but as a quiet realization that says, I cannot keep
meeting life in this way.
I do not experience surrender as giving up. I experience it as releasing
the need to force answers or outcomes. It is the moment I stop trying to
move the mountain and instead begin to notice how much energy that
effort has been costing me. Sometimes nothing on the outside changes
right away, and at times nothing changes at all, but something inside
begins to soften, and that softening matters more than we often realize.

What continues to surprise me is what comes after that softening. When
I stop bracing, there is often more clarity than I expect. Decisions begin
to feel simpler, not because they are easy, but because I trust myself
more. I listen differently, respond rather than react, and notice small
moments of beauty that I once rushed past. These shifts may seem
subtle, but they change how it feels to live inside our own lives.

For me, surrender rarely happens through thinking alone. It happens in
the body. I notice it when my breath drops lower without effort, when my
shoulders release, and when my jaw lets go on its own. Gentle
movement, stillness, and breath create the conditions for this without
demanding anything from me, and they remind my nervous system that
it is safe to stop holding everything so tightly.

If surrender feels tender or out of reach right now, I do not believe it
needs to come as a big decision or dramatic turning point. Sometimes it
looks very ordinary, like letting go of the idea that you should be further
along by now, allowing yourself to rest without explanation, or admitting
quietly that something no longer fits.

I do not believe surrender makes us passive. I believe it makes us
honest, and from that honesty comes a different kind of strength, one
that is quiet and steady, that does not need to prove anything, and that
knows when to soften and when to act. This is not about arriving
anywhere new, but about changing how we meet what is already here.
OUR WEEKLY PRACTICE:

Moving Gently Through This Week
This is not a checklist or something to get right. It is simply an invitation
you can return to once, or many times, or not at all.

      Once a day, wherever you happen to be, pause for a moment and
      place a hand somewhere grounding. Take one slow breath in and
      a slightly longer breath out, and quietly ask yourself what you
      might be holding that could loosen, even just a little, allowing the
      question itself to do the work.

      Each day, notice one small place where you are pushing, whether
      internally or externally, and see what happens if you ease off by
      just 10 percent rather than trying to fix or solve anything.

      At least once this week, let your body choose what it needs
      instead of your mind, whether that looks like rest, movement,
      quiet, or space, and see if you can trust that choice without
      second guessing it.

      On Christmas Day, or whichever day feels most charged for you
      this week, take one minute to step away, feel your feet on the
      ground, take a slow breath, and remind yourself that you are
      allowed to be exactly as you are today.

      Before sleep, choose one thing you are willing to let rest until
      tomorrow, knowing that it does not need to be resolved right now,
      and allow your body to settle.

Surrender does not mean disengaging from life. It means staying
present without force. This week does not need to be joyful,
meaningful, or peaceful to be enough. It only needs to be honest.
A Closing Thought
As this week unfolds, I hope you can meet yourself with a little more
softness than usual. There is no right way to move through this season,
and no version of it that needs to be performed. Wherever you find
yourself, however it looks or feels, is allowed. If surrender shows up
easily, welcome it. If it doesn’t, that is allowed too. Sometimes simply
noticing where we are is enough.

I’m grateful to be walking alongside you, and I trust that whatever this
week brings, you already carry what you need to meet it.

Wishing you a peaceful holiday season, in whatever form it arrives for
you.
