# The Only Moment That's Real

*By Laura Harvey*

I want to share something that I keep coming back to, not because I have mastered it, but because I haven't. Because some days I get it beautifully right, and other days I am dragged so far into the familiar stories of my past that I barely recognize I have left the present at all. That is the truth I am sitting with lately, and I think it might be sitting with you too.

There is a concept I return to again and again, in my own life and in the work I do with women in class: that catching yourself, over and over, from going unconscious in how you think, feel, and behave is actually what makes you conscious. Not arriving somewhere. Not achieving a permanent state of peace. Just the catching. The noticing. The moment you realize you have drifted, which is the moment you can choose to come back.

Because we do drift, all of us. One conversation, one memory, one small moment of feeling unseen, and suddenly we are not here anymore. We are back in an old story, feeling old feelings, reacting from a version of ourselves shaped by everything that happened before this moment. Fear, insecurity, the quiet ache of not being enough. These are not just emotions. When we live inside them long enough, they become the body's default language. The nervous system stops asking what is true now and starts simply repeating what it learned then. I know this place. It feels heavy and scrambled, and sometimes honestly a little dark.

And I also know the other place, the one that becomes available when I manage to bring myself back. When I disinvest my attention from the story I am spinning about the future, or the wound I am still turning over from the past, and I land, even briefly, in this moment, this breath, this room. That place feels like flow and clarity. Like something I can only describe as my own truth.

It takes enormous energy to make that return, and nobody tells you that. Presence is not passive. It is a practice that asks something real of you every single day. But what I have come to believe, in my bones and in the quiet of the mat, is that the effort is worth it. Because when the body finally begins to settle, when it trusts that it is safe to stop rehearsing old pain, something opens. Energy that was held in survival begins to move. And it moves toward wholeness. That, I think, is what we are really doing in restorative yoga, in meditation, in any practice that asks us to slow down and feel what is actually here. We are not escaping. We are returning. And the only place we can ever truly return to is now.

The present moment does not ask us to be perfect. It only asks us to keep coming back. And every time we do, something in us grows a little steadier, a little more awake, a little more free.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
