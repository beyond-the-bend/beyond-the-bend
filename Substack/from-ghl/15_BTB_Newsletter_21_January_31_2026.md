## Hello

Over the past little while, I’ve been living into a shift that has taken time to fully land. Not a sudden realization, but a gradual change in how I see myself and my life.

I’ve come to recognize that for a long time, I was still looking at my life through an old lens. Not old as in outdated or wrong, just no longer suited to the season I am in now.

That understanding feels settled enough now to share.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura 🩷

✨ My invitation for you this month: Notice where you’re choosing “busy” over “peace,” and gently ask yourself — is this stress actually helping me?

September arrives with its own kind of hum. For many of us, it stirs up the old rhythm of back-to-school, fresh starts, new schedules — and with it, a rush of shoulds and self-imposed pressure.

Suddenly our minds are full of lists: things to organize, tasks to complete, details that must get handled. And if we don’t tick every box, the inner critic shows up: “Why can’t I keep up?”

But here’s the thing: stress doesn’t make the work go faster. Busyness doesn’t always mean productivity. Often, it’s just noise.

What if September could feel different this year?

What if, instead of being pulled into the whirlwind, you brought a little spaciousness into your days? A pause between the tasks. A few deep breaths before the next thing. A moment to remember: peace isn’t earned after the list is done — it’s available right here, in the middle of it all.

Because when the mind is calmer, the noise fades. And when the noise fades, what truly matters has room to rise.

## A New Lens

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga


In many ways, I am still evaluating myself through a thirty or forty year old lens. A way of seeing that asks whether I am improving, keeping up, or becoming more. When I use that lens now, it can leave me feeling as though I am falling short, as though I am somehow becoming less.

What I have come to understand is that nothing is actually wrong with me.

The lens is simply wrong for this season.

A younger lens is designed for striving, proving, building, and becoming. It measures worth through momentum, achievement, and visible growth. When that same lens is carried forward into midlife, wisdom can be mistaken for loss, and stillness can be misunderstood as stagnation. What is actually depth and discernment can begin to look, through that lens, like something is missing, rather than something essential having arrived.

This season of life asks for a different way of seeing. A different lens altogether. One that does not ask what we are becoming, but instead asks what we are inhabiting.

This is not about pretending to be perfectly at peace with aging. Many of us still catch ourselves trying to look younger, revisiting old choices, or measuring ourselves against versions of who we used to be.

And yet, a new lens is available.

One that recognizes wisdom not as something we might have someday, but as something we are already standing inside. One that understands strength does not always look like pushing forward. Sometimes it looks like steadiness. Like discernment. Like knowing when effort is no longer required.

I do not think we switch lenses overnight. We notice the old one first. We feel the discomfort of it. And then, gradually, we begin to see ourselves and our lives through something truer.

When that shift happens, life does not necessarily get easier. But it becomes clearer. More coherent. We begin to recognize ourselves as capable, credible, and deeply resourced. Not diminished by time, but shaped by it. Not fading, but settling into a kind of radiance that comes from knowing who we are and trusting the ground we stand on.

When we begin to see this truth clearly, it changes how we approach love — not as something to find, earn, or chase, but as something that begins within. The way we treat ourselves becomes the soil from which every other relationship grows.

Imagine, for a moment, our inner world as a garden. Every thought, every act of care, every boundary and breath becomes part of its landscape. Some days it may look lush and alive — new growth reaching toward the sun. Other days, it may feel untended — overgrown with old habits or dry from neglect.

This garden isn’t a place to perfect. It’s a place to notice.
To be curious about what’s growing.
To acknowledge what’s struggling.
To gently tend what needs attention.

When we nurture ourselves with gentleness and honesty, that energy naturally extends outward. It softens our tone, steadies our presence, and deepens our capacity to love others without losing ourselves. But when we neglect our inner garden — through self-criticism, avoidance, or over-giving — the soil becomes depleted. Even when love comes near, it can’t quite take root.

Many of us learned long ago to earn love through doing, pleasing, or achieving. Those patterns were intelligent; they kept us connected in the ways we knew how. But as we grow older, they begin to feel heavy — as if the garden we’ve been tending was built to please everyone but ourselves.

Healing begins the moment we bring awareness to this cycle — when we pause, take a breath, and ask, What would it feel like to meet ourselves with curiosity instead of judgment?

That single question is like sunlight breaking through clouds. It doesn’t demand that we fix or change — only that we notice, and allow. Each act of awareness becomes a drop of water for the roots of self-trust. Each kind thought becomes nourishment for the soil of our hearts.

Over time, the garden shifts. We begin to see new shoots of growth — boundaries formed from clarity rather than fear, choices made from love rather than guilt, tenderness that doesn’t depend on anyone else’s approval.

We don’t need to be “fixed” or endlessly giving to be worthy of love. We only need to meet ourselves where we are — with patience, tenderness, and care.

Because when we do, our roots grow stronger. And from that fertile ground, every connection in our lives — with others, with the world, and with our own hearts — begins to bloom in a way that feels more peaceful, more authentic, and more true.

# An Invitation to Practice Together

If you’ve been feeling the pull to stay closer to home right now, you’re not alone. This season naturally asks us to turn inward, to move more slowly, and to choose what truly supports us.

At the same time, it’s often when we most need steady movement, nervous system care, and a sense of connection that reminds us we’re not doing this alone.

My online classes were created for exactly this moment.

They are not about pushing, perfecting, or keeping up. They are about moving in ways that feel intelligent and kind, settling the nervous system, and coming back into a sense of steadiness in your own body. You can join from home, move at your own pace, and still feel part of a grounded, thoughtful community of women who value depth over performance.

If you’ve been wanting a way to care for yourself that feels nourishing rather than demanding, supportive rather than overwhelming, I would love to practice with you online. These classes have become a quiet anchor for many people, and they may be exactly what you’re looking for right now too.

## Practice of the Week

This week’s practice is a simple invitation to listen and notice.

I recommend watching a TED Talk by **Connie Schultz** called *A Woman Over 50: A Life Unleashed*. Connie Schultz has spent decades as a journalist paying close attention to everyday lives and quiet turning points, and that depth of listening comes through clearly in how she speaks.

As you watch, you might hold the idea of the lens gently in mind. Notice where her words reflect a shift away from younger expectations and toward a different way of seeing. Pay attention to moments that feel familiar, affirming, or slightly relieving, as if something you have sensed privately is being named out loud.

This is not about agreeing with everything she says or taking anything on as a new belief. It is simply an opportunity to sit with a perspective that comes from lived experience and to notice how it lands for you.

If you feel drawn to it, you can watch the talk here:

## **A Closing Thought**

Seeing differently changes more than how we think. It shapes how we stand, how we move, and how we listen to ourselves.

As the lens changes, life begins to feel less like something we need to keep up with and more like something we are already a part of. There is steadiness here, and a quiet confidence that does not need to announce itself.

That shift alone may be enough for now.

With warmth and gratitude,
