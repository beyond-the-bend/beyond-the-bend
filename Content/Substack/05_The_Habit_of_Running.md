# The Habit of Running

*By Laura Harvey*

I noticed something yesterday that I keep catching myself doing. My shoulders had crept up toward my ears, my breath was shallow, and I was rushing through a series of tasks that genuinely did not need to be rushed. Nothing was on fire. There was no emergency. I was simply moving fast because everything around me felt fast, and somewhere along the way I had matched my internal speed to the speed of the world outside.

It made me pause. Why do we do this? Why do we feel obligated to match the urgency of whatever is loudest around us?

The teacher Thich Nhat Hanh spoke about something he called the habit of running, and he used the Sanskrit word vasana to describe it, which translates roughly to habit energy, the deep momentum of the past pushing us around in the present. He observed that so many of us spend our entire lives running, not just physically but internally, even when we are sitting still. We run while we are eating. We run even in our sleep. We are perpetually leaning forward into the next moment because the momentum is so strong and so practiced that we barely notice it is happening.

What this momentum tells us is that if we just hurry fast enough, we will finally catch up, and once we catch up, then we can relax. But of course we know, someplace below the rushing, that this is not true. The finish line keeps moving. There is always another list, another request, another role that needs filling. Living in a state of constant forward-lean does not actually make us more efficient. It just empties us. It steals the texture of our lives. When we rush through making dinner, we miss the smell of the spices. When we rush through a conversation, we miss the actual connection. We end up skimming the surface of our own lives at high speed, technically present but not really here at all.

What Thich Nhat Hanh taught, and what I have had to learn slowly and imperfectly for myself, is that we will never truly be caught up. And he meant this as good news. It means we do not have to wait for an empty to-do list before we allow ourselves to feel calm. We can choose to inhabit the moment we are actually in right now, with the list still unfinished and the inbox still full, and that choice is available at any point in the day.

There is something that feels almost radical about choosing to move slowly when everything else is spinning. It feels like a small rebellion, a way of saying that the pace of my inner life is not determined by whatever is loudest around me. That is not falling behind. That is deciding to actually live in the life you already have.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
