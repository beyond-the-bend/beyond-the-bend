Time Traveling Through the Heart

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

My grandmother was the one who first gave me Women Who Run With
the Wolves.
Her copy still sits on my bookshelf, worn soft at the edges, filled with her
handwriting and tiny tabs marking the passages that must have stirred
her soul. Every now and then, I take it down and read the lines she
underlined - the ones she must have felt in her bones. When I do, it
feels like I’m time-traveling, not through years or places, but through my
heart.

In her notes, I hear the younger version of her - fierce, curious, still
reaching for something unnamed. And I see my own younger self
reflected there too: the girl who wanted to be brave but also deeply
loved, the one who didn’t yet know that both could coexist.
Clarissa Pinkola Estés writes about the wild woman - that instinctive
part of us that knows when it’s time to return home to ourselves. When I
reread my grandmother’s thoughts, I realize that wildness isn’t only in
how we break free; it’s also in how we remember - how we stay loyal to
the parts of ourselves we once tucked away to survive.

This, to me, is what it means to time-travel through the heart:
to sit quietly and feel the echo of all the women we’ve ever been - the
child, the dreamer, the caretaker, the seeker - and to recognize that
each of them still lives within us. Their voices blend into a chorus of
wisdom, guiding us home to ourselves.

And when we slow down long enough to meet them, we begin to
understand that these inner versions of ourselves aren’t gone - they’re
simply waiting to be acknowledged. The tender one who learned to
please. The fiery one who once spoke too boldly and retreated in
shame. The hopeful one who still believes in magic.
When we sit beside them, with curiosity instead of criticism, something
softens. The tension between who we were and who we are begins to
ease.

It’s a kind of gentle inner reunion, a remembering that healing doesn’t
mean erasing our past selves but listening to them, holding them close,
and letting them know they still belong. Each one carries a fragment of
our aliveness, and when we welcome them back, our hearts expand.

Perhaps that’s what my grandmother knew when she passed that book
into my hands that one day, I’d read her notes not just as her story, but
as my own. That through her words, I’d learn that healing is less about
becoming someone new, and more about remembering who we’ve
always been.
💌 Practice of the Week: A Letter to Your
Future Self
There’s something extraordinary about putting your present-day heart into words,
not as a plan or a checklist, but as a small act of remembering who you are right
now.

We often wonder what our future selves might say to us, but what if we turned
that around? What if we were the ones leaving breadcrumbs of insight for the
woman we’re still becoming?

Imagine if your eight-year-old self had written you a letter. What might she have
said? What would she have dreamed of, feared, or hoped for? Or your teenage
self - full of intensity, courage, and contradiction - what would she have wanted
you to remember?

Wouldn’t it be incredible to hold those letters now, to see how far you’ve come,
how much you’ve carried, and how beautifully you’ve grown?
That’s the gift you’re giving your future self when you write to her - a record of this
very moment, this chapter of your becoming.

✍️ Write a Letter to “Future You”
Choose a moment in time - a month, a year, or even a decade from now - and
write a letter to your future self. There’s no wrong way to do this. What matters is
that it comes from your heart.

Here are some gentle questions to guide you:

      What do you most want your future self to remember about this season of
      your life?

      What truths are you beginning to see more clearly now?

      What do you hope will unfold — in your relationships, your health, your
      work, or your spirit?

      What do you want her to know when life feels uncertain?

      What would make you proud to read years from now?

You might even want to invite your younger selves into the process — imagine
how each of them would answer these questions. Let their voices mingle with
your own.

Because healing, I’ve come to believe, isn’t about becoming someone new.
It’s about finally coming home to everyone you’ve ever been. 🌿
