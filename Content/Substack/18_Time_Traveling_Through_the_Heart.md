# Time Traveling Through the Heart

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been sitting with something tender lately. I recently attended a workshop that invited me to build a relationship with my past selves, not just to remember them, but to truly meet them, to actually sit with them rather than treat them as prologue to who I am now. It was a more emotional experience than I expected it to be. It opened quiet corners of memory I did not realize were still waiting to be seen.

Since then, I have found myself reflecting on all the versions of me that have lived within this one body. The bright and curious little girl who learned to stay small and invisible. The teenager who wanted to be fearless but ended up feeling shame for shining. The woman who has learned to begin again, and again, and again. I have been wondering what they might say to me now, and what I might say back.

Somewhere in that reflection, my grandmother came to mind. She was the one who first placed Women Who Run With the Wolves in my hands, her copy, worn soft at the edges, full of her handwriting and tiny tabs marking the passages that must have stirred her soul. Every now and then I take it down and read the lines she underlined, the ones she must have felt in her bones. When I do, it feels like time traveling, not through years or places, but through my heart.

In her notes I hear the younger version of her, fierce and curious, still reaching for something she may not have had language for yet. And I see my own younger self reflected there too, the girl who wanted to be brave but also deeply loved, the one who did not yet know that both could coexist. Clarissa Pinkola Estés writes about the wild woman, that instinctive part of us that knows when it is time to return home to ourselves. When I reread my grandmother's thoughts, I realize that wildness is not only in how we break free. It is also in how we remember, in how we stay loyal to the parts of ourselves we once tucked away in order to survive.

This is what I think it means to time travel through the heart: to sit quietly and feel the echo of all the women we have ever been, the child, the dreamer, the caretaker, the seeker, and to recognize that each of them still lives within us. When we slow down long enough to meet them, with curiosity rather than criticism, something softens. The tension between who we were and who we are begins to ease. It is a kind of gentle inner reunion, a remembering that healing does not mean erasing our past selves but listening to them, holding them close, letting them know they still belong.

Perhaps that is what my grandmother knew when she passed that book into my hands. That one day I would read her notes not just as her story, but as my own. That through her words, I would learn that healing is less about becoming someone new and more about remembering who we have always been.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
