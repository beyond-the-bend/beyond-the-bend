A Buddhist Perspective That Made Space for Mercy

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

I was listening to an interview with Elizabeth Gilbert recently (one of my
absolute heroes), and she shared something that landed so deeply with
me. She spoke about a Buddhist teaching that instead of labeling things
as right or wrong, we can see them as skilled or unskilled.

I love this shift in language. It takes us out of judgment and shame, and
instead invites us into curiosity and growth.

In Buddhism:

A skilled (kusala) action is one that arises from clarity, compassion, or
wisdom. It leads to freedom from suffering and creates conditions for
peace and well-being - both for ourselves and those around us.

An unskilled (akusala) action arises from confusion, craving, fear, or
anger. It adds to our suffering, clouds the mind, and often harms our
relationships or deepens our own struggles.

What I love about this is that it reframes the whole picture. Instead of
saying, “I’m bad because I snapped at someone,” we can say, “That
was an unskilled way of handling my stress. What could be more skillful
next time?”

I’ve noticed this in my own life. Sometimes I’ll say or do something that
isn’t so kind, and my old pattern would be to beat myself up about it:
“Why did I do that? I’m a bad person.” But when I look through this
Buddhist lens, I can soften. I can say, “That was an unskilled choice, but
it doesn’t define me.” From that place, I can reflect: “If I had been in a
more skillful, compassionate space, what might I have chosen
differently?”

For me, this perspective feels so freeing. It allows me to be merciful with
myself, to remove the harsh judgment, and to stay curious about how I
want to show up next time. And it reminds me that living authentically
isn’t about being perfect - it’s about gently moving toward more skillful
choices, rooted in compassion for myself and for others.

And just as important, it changes how I see others. When someone else
acts harshly, it doesn’t mean they’re a bad person - it simply means
they’re acting from an unskilled place. That shift helps me to hold them
with more mercy, too.

For me, the beauty of this perspective is the freedom it brings. When I
can say, “That was unskilled” instead of “That was bad,” there’s space to
breathe. Judgment loosens its grip. My heart feels lighter, more open.
And in that space, I feel more alive, more free.

I invite you to play with this lens in your own life - to notice the shift that
happens when you see an action, yours or someone else’s, not as
wrong or bad, but simply as unskilled. See how it feels in your body, in
your heart. You might find that same sense of space and freedom
waiting for you, too.

🌿 Practice of the Week: From Pause to Mercy
One of the most powerful ways to live into this idea of “skilled vs.
unskilled” is to bring it into our everyday moments and then give
ourselves space to reflect with compassion. This practice blends both:
a pause you can take in real time, and a meditation you can return to
later. Together, they create a cycle of awareness, mercy, and growth.

The Skilled Response Pause (in the moment)

The next time irritation, defensiveness, or self-criticism arises, try this:

   1. Notice the spark. Catch the moment before you react.

   2. Anchor in the body. Place a hand on your heart or belly. Take
      three slow breaths.

   3. Name it gently. Silently say: “This is an unskilled impulse.”

   4. Open to possibility. Ask: “What would be a more skillful
      response here?”

   5. Choose one shift. Maybe it’s softening your tone, stepping away,
      or offering yourself a kind word.

Even if you don’t always succeed in shifting, the act of pausing plants
the seed of awareness.

Compassionate Reframing Meditation (later, in quiet)

At the end of the day, or anytime you want to reset, find a quiet place to
sit for 10–15 minutes.

   1. Close your eyes and take a few grounding breaths.

   2. Recall one of the moments from your day when you paused - or
      when you wished you had.

   3. Instead of judgment, say silently: “That was unskilled, not
      unworthy.”

   4. With each breath, offer yourself compassion:

            Inhale: “I am learning.”

            Exhale: “I choose compassion.”

   5. Imagine how you might respond more skillfully next time. See it,
   5. Imagine how you might respond more skillfully next time. See it,
      feel it, and let your body register the possibility.

   6. Close with a blessing: “May I grow in skill. May I act with clarity
      and compassion - for myself and others.”

Why it matters

By weaving the pause into your day and the meditation into your
evening, you create a gentle rhythm of awareness and reflection. Over
time, the line between the two blurs - your pauses become more
compassionate, and your meditations begin to shape your daily
choices.

It’s not about perfection. It’s about building a life where skill, mercy, and
compassion slowly become your natural way of being.
