# Coming Home to Your Body

*By Laura Harvey*

I came across a phrase recently that I had heard before but had never really paused to understand: spiritual bypassing. At first, it sounds almost appealing, almost kind, like something you would want to do. But when I looked a little closer, I realized it names something many of us have done, often with the very best of intentions.

Spiritual bypassing is what happens when we use spiritual ideas to move around our human experience instead of through it. When we reach for gratitude to push away grief before we have even let ourselves acknowledge that the grief is there. When we tell ourselves to stay positive so we do not have to feel what actually hurts. When we use concepts like acceptance or release as a way of skipping past the part where we actually feel the thing. We rise above, as the language often encourages us to, without ever really landing.

Most of us were taught, explicitly or very subtly, that difficult emotions are something to transcend. That if we meditate enough, pray enough, practice enough, we will eventually float above the messiness of being human. But the body knows better than that. The body does not bypass. It remembers, it holds, and it waits patiently for us to listen. You can tell yourself you have forgiven something when the anger is still living in your jaw and your shoulders. You can reach for gratitude while grief is still sitting in your chest waiting to be acknowledged. The body keeps the record with absolute honesty, regardless of what the mind has decided to believe.

This is where somatic work offers something profoundly different from what most of us were taught. Rather than asking us to override our experience, it invites us to enter it safely. Rather than using spiritual ideas to push feelings away, it creates space to finally let them be here, to exist without immediately being managed or meaning-made or moved past. And something surprising tends to happen in that space. When sensation, emotion, breath, and awareness are allowed to exist together without us pushing anything away or forcing meaning onto it, there is often a quiet sense of wholeness that emerges, not because anything has been fixed, but because we have stopped exiling parts of ourselves.

The women I work with are navigating real and significant transitions. Hormonal shifts that change how they feel in their own skin, identity changes that shake their sense of self, life stages that demand a new relationship with their bodies. This is not the time to use spirituality to push experience away. This is actually the time to come home to it. Sometimes the most profound thing is not rising above but coming back, back to the body, back to what is actually here, back to the wisdom that has been waiting in your own nervous system all along.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
