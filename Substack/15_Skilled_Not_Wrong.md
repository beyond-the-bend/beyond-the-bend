# Skilled, Not Wrong

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been reflecting this week on how often we are harder on ourselves than we need to be. It is so easy to slip into judgment, to carry the weight of I should have known better or why did I do that, and to let those questions become something we live inside for longer than they deserve. I was listening to an interview with Elizabeth Gilbert recently, one of my absolute heroes, and she shared something that landed so deeply with me. She spoke about a Buddhist teaching, the idea that instead of labeling our actions as right or wrong, we can see them as skilled or unskilled.

I love this shift in language. It takes us entirely out of the territory of judgment and shame and drops us instead into something that feels more spacious, curiosity and growth. In Buddhism, a skilled action, what is called kusala, is one that arises from clarity, compassion, or wisdom. It leads away from suffering and creates conditions for peace and wellbeing, both for ourselves and for the people around us. An unskilled action, akusala, arises from confusion, craving, fear, or anger. It adds to suffering, clouds the mind, and tends to harm our relationships or deepen our own struggles. But here is the thing: neither of these is a verdict on who we are as a person. They are simply observations about the state we were in when we acted.

What I love about this framework is that it reframes the whole picture without letting us off the hook. Instead of saying I am bad because I snapped at someone, we can say that was an unskilled way of handling my stress. What might be more skillful next time? It creates room for self-reflection without the punishment. And from that more spacious place, we can actually look at what happened, what we were carrying when it happened, and what might be available to us in a future moment that was not available in that one.

I have noticed this in my own life. When I act in ways that are not kind, especially toward myself, my old pattern was to beat myself up thoroughly: why did I do that, what is wrong with me, I should have been better than that. When I look through this Buddhist lens instead, I can soften. I can say that was unskilled, and it does not define me. From that place I can actually reflect rather than brace.

Just as importantly, it changes how I hold other people. When someone acts harshly, it does not mean they are a bad person. It likely means they are acting from an unskilled place, from fear or pain or exhaustion or everything they are carrying that has nothing to do with me. That shift helps me hold them with more mercy. And honestly, I think we are all a little more capable of mercy in general when we stop treating every imperfect action as evidence of character.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
