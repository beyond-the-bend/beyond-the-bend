When Muddy Water Settles:
A Practice in Not Fixing

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

There's a teaching from philosopher Alan Watts that has stayed with me
for years:

"Muddy water is best cleared by leaving it alone."

Imagine a glass of water, clouded with sediment. Our instinct is to stir it,
to do something, to make it clear through action. But every movement of
the spoon only swirls the particles back up, making the water murkier.

The only way for the water to become clear is to stop. To set the glass
down and let gravity do what our effort cannot - allow everything to
settle on its own.

This is such a profound metaphor for how many of us move through life,
especially those of us in midlife.

The Habit of Stirring
Most of us weren't taught to leave things alone. We learned early that
clarity, peace, and worthiness came through doing. Through trying
harder. Through figuring it out.

When we feel anxious, we try to think our way to calm.
When we feel uncertain, we search for answers.
When something feels uncomfortable, we immediately try to fix it.

We stir and stir and stir, believing that if we just keep moving, keep
analyzing, keep pushing, eventually the water will clear.

But it doesn't.

It just gets cloudier.

And beneath all that stirring is often a deeper fear: that if we stop, if we
rest, if we simply let things be, something terrible might happen. We
might fall behind. We might miss something important. We might
discover that we're not okay.

But here's what I've come to understand through years of practice:

The muddy water isn't a problem to solve. It's a moment asking for
stillness.

The Nervous System Connection

When our nervous system is in overdrive - juggling responsibilities,
managing emotions, anticipating what's next - it's like constantly shaking
that glass of water. The system stays activated, the thoughts keep
swirling, and clarity remains just out of reach.

This is especially true for women in midlife. We've spent decades in
motion - caring for others, building careers, holding families together.
Our nervous systems learned long ago that staying busy meant staying
safe. Stopping felt dangerous.

But now, in this season of life, our bodies are asking us to reconsider.

The fatigue we feel isn't laziness. The brain fog isn't failure. The
emotional sensitivity isn't weakness.

It's our system saying: Please. Stop stirring.

This season of life isn't a punishment, it's an invitation. An Invitation to
finally set the glass down. To let the sediment of decades settle. To
discover what becomes visible when we stop trying so hard to see.

What Stillness Reveals
When we give ourselves permission to stop stirring, even for a few
breaths, something subtle begins to shift.

The thoughts still come, but they lose some of their urgency.
The emotions still move through, but they don't demand immediate
action.
The body softens, just slightly, as if remembering something it once
knew.

This isn't about forcing calm or "doing stillness right." It's simply about
creating the conditions for clarity to emerge on its own.

And often, what we discover in that settling is this:

We were never as unclear as we thought.
We were never as broken as we feared.
We were simply stirring too much to see what was already there.

The Invitation

As December unfolds and the pace around us quickens, what if we
practiced something radically different?

What if, instead of trying to figure everything out, we simply let some
things be?

What if we trusted that the answers we're seeking might arrive not
through effort, but through rest?

What if we remembered that muddy water clears itself when we finally
give it permission to settle?

You don't need to have it all figured out right now.
You don't need to stir harder.
You don't need to be clearer, calmer, or more together than you are.

You only need to set the glass down.

And breathe.

The water will clear on its own.
Practice of the Week:

Settling the Water
This week's practice is beautifully simple. You don't need props,
preparation, or perfect conditions. Just a few quiet minutes and a
willingness to let things be.

Find a comfortable seat. Let your body settle into the support beneath
you.

Close your eyes or soften your gaze.

Take a slow breath in through your nose, feeling the coolness of the air
as it enters.

Exhale gently through your mouth, as if you're releasing something
you've been holding.

Now, imagine a glass of water in front of you. See the sediment swirling
inside - cloudy, murky, unclear.

This glass represents your mind, your nervous system, your inner world
in this moment. All the thoughts, worries, plans, and feelings stirring
together.

Don't try to fix it. Don't try to clear it.

Simply watch it.

With each inhale, imagine yourself becoming still, like the hand that
stops stirring.

With each exhale, imagine the particles beginning to settle - slowly,
gradually, without force.

You don't need to make this happen. You're simply creating the
condition for clarity to emerge on its own.

Continue breathing slowly for several minutes, watching the water in
your mind's eye become just a little clearer with each breath.

Notice how your body feels.
Notice the space that opens when you stop trying so hard.

When you're ready, open your eyes.
