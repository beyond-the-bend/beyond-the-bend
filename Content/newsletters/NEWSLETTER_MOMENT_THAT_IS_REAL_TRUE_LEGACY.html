This Week's Wellness Wisdom

The Only Moment That's Real

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

There's a concept I return to again and again, in my own life and in the work I do with women in class: that catching yourself, over and over, from going unconscious in how you think, feel, and behave is actually what makes you conscious. Not arriving somewhere. Not achieving a permanent state of peace. Just the catching. The noticing. The moment you realize you've drifted.

Because we do drift. All of us.

One conversation, one memory, one small moment of feeling unseen, and suddenly we're not here anymore. We're back in an old story, feeling old feelings, reacting from a version of ourselves shaped by everything that happened before this moment. Fear. Insecurity. The quiet ache of not-enough. These aren't just emotions. When we live inside them long enough, they become the body's default language. The nervous system stops asking what's true now and starts simply repeating what it learned then.

I know this place. It feels heavy. Scrambled. Sometimes honestly a little dark.

And I also know the other place, the one that becomes available when I manage to bring myself back. When I disinvest my attention from the story I'm spinning about the future, or the wound I'm still turning over from the past, and I land, even briefly, in this moment. This breath. This room. This body, right now.

That place feels like flow. Like clarity. Like something I can only describe as my truth.

It takes enormous energy to make that return. Nobody tells you that. Presence isn't passive. It's a practice that asks something real of you, every single day. But what I've come to believe, in my bones and in the quiet of the mat, is that the effort is worth it. Because when the body finally begins to settle, when it trusts that it's safe to stop rehearsing old pain, something opens. Energy that was held in survival begins to move. And it moves toward wholeness.

That's what I think we're really doing in restorative yoga. In meditation. In any practice that asks us to slow down and feel what's actually here.

We're not escaping. We're returning.

And the only place we can ever truly return to is now.

OUR WEEKLY PRACTICE:

Coming Back

This is a practice you can do anywhere, at any moment you notice you've drifted.

  • Connect: Find a comfortable seat or simply pause wherever you are. Let your hands rest. Let your face soften.
  • Breathe: Take one slow breath in through your nose. Exhale gently, as if you are setting something down.
  • Notice: Now ask yourself, quietly and without judgment: where am I right now? Not physically, but inside. Are you here, in this moment? Or have you slipped somewhere else, into a worry, a memory, a story about what's coming?
  • Return: Simply notice. No criticism. No urgency to fix anything. Then take another breath and let it bring you back. Not to a perfect state of calm, but simply to now. To this room. To this body. To this one moment, which is the only moment that is ever actually real.

Each return is not a failure. Each return is the practice itself.

A Closing Thought

The present moment doesn't ask us to be perfect. It only asks us to keep coming back.

And every time we do, something in us grows a little steadier, a little more awake, a little more free.

Thank you for exploring this with me.
