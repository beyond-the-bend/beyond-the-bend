# Swimming With the River

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been rereading a book this week that I return to whenever I need to be reminded of something I already know but keep forgetting. It is called Talking Zen by Alan Watts, and it is one of those collections that you can open to almost any page and find something that stops you mid-breath. Watts had a way of making the most profound ideas feel like the most obvious ones, as though he were simply pointing at something that had been sitting in plain sight all along and saying, there, do you see it now.

There is a passage in particular that I have underlined and come back to so many times that the page almost falls open on its own. He writes about the Tao, the fundamental flow of everything, and he compares it to a tremendous river that carries us all along whether we know it or not. Some people swim against the river, he says, and yet they are still being carried. Others have learned to swim with it. They are carried along too, but they are aware of it. The ones swimming against the current think they are going in the opposite direction, though they are not. Not really.

What strikes me most is what he says next. The person who gives up that pretense, who realizes they are inevitably carried by the river and chooses to swim with it, suddenly acquires behind everything they do the force of the river itself. I wrote "great analogy" in the margin the first time I read that, and honestly it still takes my breath away.

I think about who I was in my twenties, and I recognize someone who was swimming hard against the current and calling it ambition. I thought life was something I had to make happen through sheer force of will. I gripped everything so tightly, my plans, my relationships, my ideas about how things were supposed to go. If something was not working I pushed harder, convinced that the answer to every problem was more effort. And when the effort did not produce the result I wanted, I blamed myself for not trying hard enough. That is an exhausting way to live, and I lived that way for a very long time.

What I did not understand then is that the river was carrying me the whole time. Even when I was thrashing against it, even when I was convinced I was failing, the current was still moving. Life was still unfolding. The things I was so desperately trying to control were already in motion, and most of the suffering I felt came not from what was actually happening but from my insistence that it should be happening differently.

I am in my fifties now, and something has shifted that I do not think I could have rushed even if I had wanted to. I have stopped trying to force the river to go where I think it should. Not because I have given up or because I no longer care about where I am headed. It is the opposite, actually. I care more deeply than I ever have. But I have learned that the caring does not require the gripping. I can have a direction without needing to control every turn. I can move with intention and still allow life to carry me where it needs to.

This is what surrender actually looks like, and it is nothing like what I used to think it was. I used to believe that surrender meant quitting, lying down, letting the world walk over you. But real surrender is something entirely different. It is the moment you stop exhausting yourself fighting what is already in motion and you let the force of the river move through what you do instead. It is not weakness. It is the most powerful thing I have ever experienced.

In my yoga practice and in my teaching, I see this constantly. The students who grip and force their way into a pose are the ones who struggle the most. The ones who breathe into it, who allow the body to open rather than demanding that it comply, are the ones who find the depth they were looking for. It is the same principle, just lived through the body instead of through philosophy. The river is always there, in our breath, in our movement, in the quiet moments between all the doing.

If you are someone who has spent years swimming against the current, I want you to know something. The exhaustion you feel is not a sign that you are not trying hard enough. It might be a sign that you have been trying too hard at the wrong thing. What would it feel like to stop fighting the water for just a moment and let it hold you? Not forever, not recklessly, but just long enough to notice that it was carrying you all along.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
