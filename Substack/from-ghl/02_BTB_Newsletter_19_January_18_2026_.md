September arrives with its own kind of hum. For many of us, it stirs up the old rhythm of back-to-school, fresh starts, new schedules — and with it, a rush of shoulds and self-imposed pressure.

Suddenly our minds are full of lists: things to organize, tasks to complete, details that must get handled. And if we don’t tick every box, the inner critic shows up: “Why can’t I keep up?”

But here’s the thing: stress doesn’t make the work go faster. Busyness doesn’t always mean productivity. Often, it’s just noise.

What if September could feel different this year?

What if, instead of being pulled into the whirlwind, you brought a little spaciousness into your days? A pause between the tasks. A few deep breaths before the next thing. A moment to remember: peace isn’t earned after the list is done — it’s available right here, in the middle of it all.

Because when the mind is calmer, the noise fades. And when the noise fades, what truly matters has room to rise.

## The Wisdom in Discomfort

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga


**The Invitation to Stay**

Life invites us to stay. To pause inside the unsettled places instead of rushing past them.

This isn't easy territory. Many of us learned that motion equals safety. That stillness means something is wrong. But I'm finding that some of the most important shifts happen when we allow ourselves to remain with what feels uncomfortable, without needing it to resolve right away.

For much of my life, discomfort felt like a call to immediate action. A signal that something needed to change *now*. My nervous system learned early that staying still inside discomfort wasn't safe. So I became good at moving. Deciding. Fixing. Pushing forward. Even when things looked calm on the outside, there was often urgency underneath.

What I've come to understand is that this urgency wasn't intuition. It was habit. It was a nervous system that believed relief only came through motion.

**What Happens When We Stay**

When we actually allow ourselves to feel discomfort without immediately reacting to it, something surprising happens. The intensity softens, not because we made it soften, but because it was allowed to complete its own cycle. We start to notice that discomfort has texture and shape. It rises. It peaks. It settles. And often, underneath it, there's something much quieter waiting to be felt.

This has changed how I understand purpose.

I used to believe purpose would feel motivating, energizing, clear like a strong yes in the body that pushes us forward. And sometimes it does feel like that. But more often, purpose feels subtle. Almost shy. It shows up when we stop demanding certainty and start listening instead.

Purpose doesn't always arrive as confidence. Sometimes it arrives as a gentle steadiness. A sense of being rooted even when the mind is unsure. A feeling of rightness that doesn't need to be defended or explained.

**The Doorway**

Discomfort, it turns out, is often the doorway not because it's pleasant, but because it slows us down enough to notice what's actually true. When we stop trying to escape it, we can hear ourselves more clearly. Not the voice that panics or rushes, but the one that's been quietly present all along.

When we begin to see this truth clearly, it changes how we approach love — not as something to find, earn, or chase, but as something that begins within. The way we treat ourselves becomes the soil from which every other relationship grows.

Imagine, for a moment, our inner world as a garden. Every thought, every act of care, every boundary and breath becomes part of its landscape. Some days it may look lush and alive — new growth reaching toward the sun. Other days, it may feel untended — overgrown with old habits or dry from neglect.

This garden isn’t a place to perfect. It’s a place to notice.
To be curious about what’s growing.
To acknowledge what’s struggling.
To gently tend what needs attention.

When we nurture ourselves with gentleness and honesty, that energy naturally extends outward. It softens our tone, steadies our presence, and deepens our capacity to love others without losing ourselves. But when we neglect our inner garden — through self-criticism, avoidance, or over-giving — the soil becomes depleted. Even when love comes near, it can’t quite take root.

Many of us learned long ago to earn love through doing, pleasing, or achieving. Those patterns were intelligent; they kept us connected in the ways we knew how. But as we grow older, they begin to feel heavy — as if the garden we’ve been tending was built to please everyone but ourselves.

Healing begins the moment we bring awareness to this cycle — when we pause, take a breath, and ask, What would it feel like to meet ourselves with curiosity instead of judgment?

That single question is like sunlight breaking through clouds. It doesn’t demand that we fix or change — only that we notice, and allow. Each act of awareness becomes a drop of water for the roots of self-trust. Each kind thought becomes nourishment for the soil of our hearts.

Over time, the garden shifts. We begin to see new shoots of growth — boundaries formed from clarity rather than fear, choices made from love rather than guilt, tenderness that doesn’t depend on anyone else’s approval.

We don’t need to be “fixed” or endlessly giving to be worthy of love. We only need to meet ourselves where we are — with patience, tenderness, and care.

Because when we do, our roots grow stronger. And from that fertile ground, every connection in our lives — with others, with the world, and with our own hearts — begins to bloom in a way that feels more peaceful, more authentic, and more true.

## Practice of the Week

**Learning to Stay**

This is a practice I have been returning to myself. I am sharing it because it has helped me, not because I have mastered it.

Find a comfortable place to sit or lie down. Let your body settle in its own way. There's no need to arrange yourself into a particular position. Just let yourself land where you are.

Notice your breath just as it is. Not trying to deepen it or control it. Simply noticing the natural rhythm that's already there.

Bring your attention to an area of your body that feels mildly uncomfortable. Nothing intense or overwhelming. Just something you can feel: perhaps tension in your shoulders, tightness in your chest, a flutter in your belly, or heaviness in your throat.

Choose something manageable. This practice is not about pushing yourself into what feels too big. It's about learning to stay with what's present without needing to fix it or make it go away.

Instead of trying to understand the sensation, analyze where it came from, or figure out what it means, quietly say to yourself: *I can stay.*

Let your attention rest there gently for three slow breaths. You're not trying to dissolve the sensation or breathe it away. You're simply keeping it company.

Notice what happens. Sometimes the sensation shifts or softens. Sometimes it stays exactly the same. Sometimes it intensifies briefly before settling. All of these responses are normal.

What seems to matter most is not what the sensation does, but that I do not abandon myself in the middle of it.

When it feels complete, and you'll sense when that moment arrives, gently widen your awareness back to your whole body. Notice your breath again. Feel the surface beneath you. Hear the sounds in the room around you.

Take a moment before you move back into your day.

This practice has been less about changing how I feel and more about building trust that I can remain present with myself. That I don't need to rush toward relief. That discomfort can be felt without becoming an emergency.

It's a small practice, but it's teaching me something important: that steadiness is possible even when certainty is not.

## **A Closing Thought**

I do not feel like I am on the other side of this yet. I am very much in it. But what I am beginning to sense is that staying with discomfort is not a failure or a delay. It might actually be part of the process of becoming more honest with myself.

If purpose feels fuzzy or distant right now, I am discovering that it may not be something to chase. It may be something that meets us when we are willing to stay exactly where we are, even when that place feels unfinished.
