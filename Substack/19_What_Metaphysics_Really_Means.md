# What Metaphysics Really Means

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been doing some reading lately, nothing too heavy, just something that stirred up an old word from a much earlier chapter of my life: metaphysics.

It is a word I first met in university when I was completing my philosophy degree. Back then it lived in lecture halls and long essays and theories about being and consciousness. The ideas themselves stirred something in me, but the way they were presented never quite sat right. It was the tone, I think, the certainty of it, the sense of authority. A room full of very confident voices explaining the unexplainable, as though truth were something you could pin down with the perfect argument. There was very little humility in it, very little humanity, and even in the moments that were genuinely beautiful, something felt missing. I did not have the words for it then, but I do now: they were circling the truth from the outside rather than stepping into it.

Reading this week brought all of that back, not as irritation but as a kind of recognition. A clarity about how different truth feels when it is lived rather than studied. How it shows up in breath, in awareness, in the quiet space inside the body that philosophy, for all its precision, never quite managed to touch.

The lived version of metaphysics feels much simpler to me. It begins the moment you notice there is a part of you that can watch your thoughts instead of becoming them. It is the quiet shift from I am thinking to I am aware of thinking. It is the softening that comes with a single breath, not because breath is magical, but because slowing down reveals layers of experience that constant thinking usually covers over. It is the deeper knowing that rises when the mind finally stops performing, not intuition exactly, but the steady presence beneath your thoughts, the part of you that does not come and go.

When I watch a thought rise and fall in meditation, I feel the difference between what changes and what stays steady beneath it. That becomes a truth felt in the body rather than argued in the mind. When a single breath shifts something inside me, I understand what philosophy was reaching for when it suggested that we are never fixed but always unfolding. My body shows me what the theories were trying to name. When stillness opens a deeper layer of knowing, I sense that reality has dimensions beneath thought, something quieter and more honest waiting below the surface.

And when I linger in the sacred gap at the end of an exhale, that tiny effortless pause where the breath has completely gone but the next one has not yet arrived, I finally understand what philosophy was trying to touch when it spoke of pure being. Philosophy circled those truths with ideas. Yoga and meditation let me step into them. The inquiry I began decades ago in those lecture halls is still the same inquiry. It just lives in my body now rather than in theories.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
