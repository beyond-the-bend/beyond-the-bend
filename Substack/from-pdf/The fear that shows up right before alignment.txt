When the gatekeeper appears

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

As I’ve been reading The Alchemist, something about the way it speaks
about fear has been staying with me. Not as an idea to understand, but
as something that’s been quietly reshaping how I recognize certain
moments in my own life.

One of the things the book points toward is that fear doesn’t always
appear at the beginning of a journey. Sometimes it shows up much later,
when something already feels close. Real. Life-altering, even. And once
I saw that, I couldn’t unsee it.

This kind of fear feels different from panic or uncertainty. It feels
reasonable. Measured. It asks questions that sound thoughtful and
protective. Why now. Why disrupt what’s working. Isn’t this enough.

In The Alchemist, fear isn’t framed as something to conquer. It appears
more like a gatekeeper, standing at a threshold. And what’s been
striking for me is realizing how often, in the past, I’ve mistaken that
gatekeeper for a warning sign. I’ve taken its presence as evidence that I
shouldn’t move forward, rather than as a signal that something
meaningful was about to change.

What I’m noticing now is that this gatekeeper tends to appear only when
something inside has already begun to align. After the fantasizing phase
has passed. After there’s a quieter, steadier sense of yes, often felt in
the body before it’s clear in words. The fear doesn’t erase that yes. It
rises alongside it.

This feels especially true in midlife. Earlier fears often came from not
knowing enough. This kind of fear comes from knowing exactly what’s at
stake. Knowing what it takes to rebuild. Knowing how much energy
change requires. Knowing how hard-won peace can be. So the fear
doesn’t shout. It whispers. And it often sounds like self-protection.

What’s been shifting for me is learning not to run from that fear or
immediately obey it. Instead, I’m practicing staying with it. Sitting in the
discomfort. Letting the fear be present without treating it as a verdict.
Seeing it not as a message to abandon myself, but as a sign that
something important and possibly very beautiful is trying to come
through.

I don’t have this mastered. I’m very much living it. But this reframe has
changed how I meet those moments. The gatekeeper no longer feels
like the end of the road. It feels like the place where something honest
asks for courage, patience, and trust.

I can’t help but wonder how often this same fear appears for other
women too, right at the edge of a real shift. Not as a sign to stop, but as
an invitation to stay long enough to see what’s on the other side.

That feels worth sitting with.

OUR WEEKLY PRACTICE:

Meeting the Gatekeeper in Real Time
At some point this week, you may notice that familiar, reasonable fear
show up. The one that sounds thoughtful. Protective. The one that asks
you to pause, delay, or stay where you are.

When you notice it, try this.

First, don’t argue with it.
Don’t try to talk yourself out of the fear or rush yourself into courage.
Simply pause.

Then, gently bring your attention into the body.

Notice where the fear is registering physically.
Is it in the chest, the belly, the throat, the shoulders?
Does it feel tight, heavy, fluttery, warm, or still?

Let yourself stay with that sensation for a few breaths, without needing
it to change. You’re not trying to calm it or interpret it. You’re simply
letting it be felt.

Now, silently acknowledge it with a simple phrase, something like:
“I see you.”
or
“You’re here.”

Then ask yourself one quiet question, not to answer immediately, but
just to let it land:
“What is this fear protecting?”
or
“What feels important right now?”

You don’t need clarity. You don’t need a plan.
The practice is simply staying present long enough not to run.

After a few moments, gently return to whatever you were doing,
carrying the awareness that the gatekeeper has been met, not obeyed
or dismissed.

You can repeat this practice anytime the fear shows up again. Each
time, you’re building trust in your ability to stay.

Why this practice matters
This isn’t about overcoming fear.
It’s about changing your relationship to it.

You’re learning to recognize the gatekeeper not as a stop sign, but as a
signal that something meaningful is near, and that you are capable of
standing at the threshold without abandoning yourself.

That, quietly, is the work.




A Closing Thought
If fear is showing up at the edge of something in your life, perhaps it
doesn’t need to be solved or silenced. Perhaps it’s simply asking to be
felt and understood. You can take your time with that. The gate will still
be there when you’re ready.
