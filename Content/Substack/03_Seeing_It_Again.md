# Seeing It Again

*By Laura Harvey*

I want to tell you something that feels almost strange to admit, because it is new for me and I am still a little surprised by it. For the first time in a long time, maybe the first time ever, I am not waiting for my life to begin. I am actually in it, seeing it, feeling it. And the difference is something I want to try to put into words, because I think you might know exactly what I mean.

I spent a lot of years not seeing my life, not because it was not beautiful, because it was, but because my attention had a habit of drifting toward what was missing, what still needed fixing, what was coming next. And when your attention lives there, the life that is actually in front of you slowly fades into the background. The cup you drink from every morning, the body that carries you through your day, the people who have been beside you for years. They do not disappear, they just stop registering. They become part of the wallpaper of your days, present but unfelt. I know this place, and I have lived there for long stretches of my life. I say that without judgment, toward myself or toward you, because it is just what happens when we are tired, when we are overwhelmed, when we have been moving so fast for so long that slowing down starts to feel dangerous.

What I have noticed lately, and I say noticed because it genuinely surprised me, is that something has shifted. I am not sure exactly when it happened. But I find myself pausing in the middle of ordinary moments and just receiving them. A cup of tea that is exactly the right temperature. The particular quality of winter light through my window in the morning. The feeling of my feet on the floor before the day begins. None of it is extraordinary, and that is almost the point. Because I think that is where most of life actually lives, not in the peak moments, not in the arrivals, not in the things we have been working toward, but in the quiet and unremarkable texture of an ordinary Tuesday that we almost let slip by without really feeling it.

This is what I think yoga and meditation are really asking us to practice, not flexibility or stillness for its own sake, but contact. Real, unhurried contact with whatever is actually here. Because when we finally let what is in front of us land, something shifts. Not dramatically, but the day feels different, fuller somehow, like we have been eating without tasting and someone finally reminded us to slow down and actually eat.

I am not saying I have mastered this. I am saying I am in a season where it is coming more naturally, and I am aware enough to know that will not always be the case. So I am paying attention while I can. I am letting things land. I am looking at my life like it is something I get to have, rather than something I am always trying to improve. And it turns out, when I do that, it is more than enough. It always was.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura

*Laura Harvey teaches yoga at Rising Moon Studio in Sherwood Park, AB. If this resonated with you, I would love to hear from you.*
