# Your Nervous System Isn't a Problem

*By Laura Harvey*

This is where I want to begin: you do not need to fix your nervous system. You need to understand what it is asking for.

So often, we feel overwhelmed, burnt out, anxious, or stuck, and we blame ourselves for it. We tell ourselves to just calm down, to get it together, to be stronger than we are managing to be in this particular moment. But what if those symptoms are not signs of failure? What if they are signs of wisdom?

Your nervous system is a messenger. It is not broken. It is communicating, and it has been communicating for a very long time, often about things we have not yet given ourselves permission to look at directly. When you are constantly in fight, flight, freeze, or fawn, it is usually because something inside you does not feel safe, and safety here is not only physical. It is emotional, relational, even spiritual. It is about whether you feel seen, heard, held, and resourced. Those are not small things. Those are the things that allow a person to actually rest.

In midlife, especially with hormonal shifts, changing identities, and years of caregiving behind us, the nervous system gets louder. The old ways of coping that worked before start to feel like they cost too much. The body begins asking for something different, and we often interpret that request as a crisis or a failing rather than what it actually is: an honest signal that the current way of doing things is no longer sustainable.

Louder does not mean broken. It means it is time to listen differently.

When I invite students to slow their breath, to place a hand on their chest or belly, to feel even one moment of physical settling before moving on to the next thing, what I am really offering is this: the experience of being with yourself without immediately trying to change what you find there. That is different from what most of us learned. Most of us learned that discomfort required a response, that restlessness needed to be managed, that the body's signals were problems to solve rather than information to receive. The practice of slowing down is, at its heart, a practice of learning to listen to yourself with curiosity instead of criticism.

The more we listen instead of override, the more the nervous system begins to trust that we are not just surviving, that it is actually safe to soften. That is the shift. And it is available, not at the end of a long healing journey, but in the next breath, if you choose to take it slowly.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
