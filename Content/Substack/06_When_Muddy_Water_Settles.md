# When Muddy Water Settles

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been catching myself lately in a very familiar pattern. Something feels unclear, a decision, a feeling, a direction I cannot quite make out, and immediately my mind begins its work. Analyzing, sorting, trying to think my way toward clarity. I will spend twenty minutes circling the same thought and arrive back exactly where I started, only more tired. All that stirring, and nothing clears.

Alan Watts said something about this that has stayed with me for a long time. He said that muddy water is best cleared by leaving it alone. Such simple wisdom, and so completely counter to everything most of us have been taught. We live in a world that insists clarity comes from effort, from doing more, figuring it out, pushing through. But what if the act of stirring is exactly what keeps the water cloudy?

Imagine a glass of water, full of sediment. The instinct is to do something, to reach in and move the particles around until the water clears by force. But every movement of the spoon just swirls everything back up. The only way for the water to actually become clear is to set the glass down and let gravity do what our effort cannot, which is to allow everything to settle on its own.

Most of us were not taught to leave things alone. We learned early that clarity and peace came through doing, through trying harder, through figuring it out before something went wrong. So when we feel anxious, we try to think our way to calm. When we feel uncertain, we search frantically for answers. When something feels uncomfortable, we immediately try to fix it. We stir and stir, believing that sustained effort is what will eventually clear the water. But beneath all of that stirring is often a quieter fear: that if we stop, if we rest, if we simply let something be for a moment, we might fall behind or miss something important or discover that we are not as okay as we need to appear.

What I have come to understand through years of practice is that the muddy water is not a problem to solve. It is a moment asking for stillness. When we give ourselves even a few minutes of not-stirring, something subtle shifts. The thoughts still come but they lose some of their urgency. The emotions still move through but they do not demand immediate action. The body softens just slightly, as if it is remembering something it always knew. And often, what we find in that settling is that we were never as unclear as we thought. We were simply stirring too hard to see what was already there.

As December unfolds and the pace around us quickens, I keep returning to this: we do not need to have it all figured out. We do not need to stir harder. We only need, for a few minutes, to set the glass down. The water will clear on its own.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
