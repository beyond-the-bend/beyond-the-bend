I’ve been doing some reading lately, nothing too heavy, just something
that stirred up an old word from a much earlier chapter of my life:
metaphysics.

It’s a word I first met in university when I was completing my philosophy
degree. Back then, it lived in lecture halls and long essays and theories
about being and consciousness, and while the ideas themselves stirred
something in me, the way they were presented never sat quite right.

It was the tone, I think - the certainty - the sense of authority.

A line of men explaining the unexplainable with great confidence, as
though truth were something they could pin down with the perfect
argument. There was very little humility in it. Very little humanity. Even in
the moments that were beautiful, something felt missing.

I didn’t have the words for it then, but I do now:

They were circling the truth, but refusing to feel it.
They analyzed truth from every angle, but never stepped into it.

As I was reading this week, all of that came back - not as irritation, but
as a kind of clarity. A recognition of how different truth feels when it’s
lived rather than studied. How it shows up in breath, in awareness, in
the quiet space inside the body that philosophy never quite managed to
touch.

That’s what inspired this week’s newsletter: a softer, more human look
at metaphysics - one that belongs in lived experience, not in abstract
explanations.

I hope it resonates with you the way it’s been resonating with me.

What Metaphysics Really Means

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

In philosophy, metaphysics asks questions like:

What is real?
What is the self?
What is consciousness?
What exists beneath everything we see?

But I believe the lived version feels simpler.

Metaphysics begins the moment you notice there is a part of you that
can watch your thoughts instead of becoming them. It is the quiet shift
from “I am thinking” to “I am aware of thinking.”

It is the softening that comes with a single breath, not because breath is
magical, but because slowing down reveals layers of experience that
constant thinking usually covers.

It is the deeper knowing that rises when the mind finally stops
performing. Not intuition exactly, but the steady presence beneath your
thoughts, the part of you that does not come and go.

It is the sense that life has more than one layer: the outer world of doing
and the inner world of being. Metaphysics is simply the curiosity that
awakens when you realize both are real.

Philosophy offers the questions.
Yoga and meditation let you feel the answers.

When I look back now, I can see that this path is not new. It is the same
inquiry I began decades ago, only now it lives in my body rather than in
theories.

Here is what I mean:

When I watch a thought rise and fall in meditation, I feel the difference
between what changes and what stays steady beneath it. It becomes a
truth that is felt rather than debated.

When a single breath shifts something inside me, I understand what
philosophy was reaching for when it suggested that we are never fixed
but always unfolding. My body shows me what the theories were trying
to name.

When stillness opens a deeper layer of knowing, I sense that reality has
dimensions beneath thought, something quieter and more honest
waiting below the surface.

When presence settles and I feel the part of me that does not sway with
every emotion, I understand being not as an idea but as a ground I can
rest in.

And when I linger in the sacred gap at the end of an exhale, that tiny
effortless pause where nothing is happening and yet everything feels
profoundly alive, I finally understand what philosophy was trying to
touch when it spoke of pure being, the essence beneath all movement.

Philosophy circled these truths with ideas.
Yoga and meditation allow me to step into them.

Practice of the Week:

The Sacred Gap
Here is a gentle practice to explore the quiet space that lives beneath thinking,
doing, and effort. This is the space where presence becomes unmistakably real.

Find a comfortable seat and allow your body to settle.
Let your shoulders fall away from your ears. Let your face soften. Let your hands
rest without effort.

Take a slow inhale through your nose, feeling the breath rise and expand.

Exhale gently, as if you are letting something inside you soften.
No pushing. No shaping. Just a natural release.

At the bottom of the exhale, allow yourself to pause.
This is the sacred gap.
It is the small, effortless stillness that appears only when the breath has
completely gone and before the next breath begins.

There is nothing to manage here.
Nothing to hold.
Nothing to create.

Simply let yourself hover in that quiet moment where the body is still, the breath is
absent, and the mind loosens its grip.

Notice the texture of this space.
Is it spacious?
Steady?
Quiet?
Does it feel as though the world has softened?

Feel how the edges of your thoughts become less dominant.
Feel how you become more aware of what remains: the steady presence beneath
movement, emotion, and breath.

Then let the next inhale arrive on its own.
Do not reach for it.
Let it come to you gently, like a tide returning to shore.

Repeat this for several rounds.
Each time you reach the bottom of the exhale, rest in that silent opening.
Let the gap widen slightly, not by holding your breath, but by easing into the
naturally occurring stillness.

Notice what happens inside you when you give yourself permission to linger here.
For many people, this pause begins to feel like a homecoming, a return to a place
inside that is untouched by urgency, noise, or expectation.
Let this be enough.
Let this be the practice.

The sacred gap has a way of revealing something honest and grounding.
A truth that does not need to be named or analyzed.
A truth that can only be felt in stillness, in presence, and in the soft spaces
between breaths.
