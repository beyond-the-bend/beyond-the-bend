Spiritual bypassing.

The Body Knows Better

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

Most of us were taught, explicitly or subtly, that difficult emotions are
something to transcend. That if we meditate enough, pray enough,
practice enough, we'll somehow float above the messiness of being
human.

But the body knows better.

The body doesn't bypass. It remembers. It holds. It waits patiently for us
to listen.

We reach for "everything happens for a reason" to avoid feeling the
devastation of what happened. We force ourselves into forgiveness to
push away the anger that's still coursing through our nervous system.
We tell ourselves that "spiritual people" don't feel rage or resentment,
using that belief to suppress what's building in our jaw, our shoulders,
our belly.

We use gratitude like a blanket to smother grief. We use positivity like a
wall to block out pain. We use spiritual concepts to exile the parts of
                p             p             p             p
ourselves we've been taught are too messy, too human, too much.

Here's what I've learned through years of somatic practice: You can't
think your way past what your body is holding. And you can't spiritually
transcend what you've refused to feel.

This is where somatic work offers something profoundly different.
Rather than asking us to override our experience, it invites us to enter it
safely. Rather than using spiritual ideas to push feelings away, we
create space to finally let them be here. Rather than chasing
transcendence, we practice the kind of presence that allows us to feel
without being overwhelmed.

And something surprising happens there.

When we allow sensation, emotion, breath, and awareness to exist
together without pushing anything away or forcing meaning onto it, there
is often a quiet sense of wholeness that emerges. Not because we've
fixed anything. But because we've stopped exiling parts of ourselves.

Your anger lives in your body. So does your grief, your joy, your
exhaustion, your power. Spiritual bypassing uses spiritual ideas to push
all of that away. Somatic practice creates the safety to finally let it in.

The women I work with are navigating profound transitions. Hormonal
shifts that change how they feel in their own skin, identity changes that
shake their sense of self, life stages that demand a new relationship
with their bodies. This isn't the time to use spirituality to push your
experience away. This is the time to come home to it.

When you breathe deeply and feel your body or feet on the ground,
you're not just doing a relaxation technique. You're reconnecting mind,
body, emotion, and spirit. You're telling your nervous system it's safe
enough to stop pushing things away. You're accessing the wisdom your
body has been holding all along.

In that way, this work isn't anti-spiritual at all. It's deeply spiritual.
Because it reunites body, mind, and spirit, not by pushing the body's
messages away, but by honoring them as part of the conversation.

Sometimes the most profound grounding comes not from rising above
but from coming home.

A Closing Thought
If you've ever felt like you were doing "all the right things" and still felt
disconnected, heavy, or unsettled, there's nothing wrong with you. You
may simply be ready for a different doorway.

One that doesn't rush you. One that doesn't demand positivity. One that
welcomes the whole of you.

The spiritual path isn't about transcending your body. It's about finally
trusting it enough to listen.

Your body knows what needs to be felt. Your nervous system knows
what needs to be processed. The practice is creating enough safety to
let that happen.

As always, take what resonates. Leave the rest. And trust that your
body, wise, patient, and honest, knows the pace.
