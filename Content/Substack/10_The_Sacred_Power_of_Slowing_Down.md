# The Sacred Power of Slowing Down

*By Laura Harvey*

There is a Zen proverb that has been sitting with me this week: you should sit in meditation for twenty minutes a day, unless you are too busy, in which case you should sit for an hour. I keep returning to it because it points at something true about the relationship between busyness and the very thing we most need, which is that the more stretched and overwhelmed we are, the more we resist the one thing that would actually help.

If you are like most of the women I work with, you have been moving at high speed for a long time. Juggling schedules, supporting others, reinventing yourself in ways both quiet and significant. The healing work you may have started, the yoga practice, the journaling, the therapy, may even feel like another thing to do properly. Another thing to keep up with. Another space where you might not be doing it quite right.

But what if the very things that feel like problems, the fatigue, the brain fog, the emotional sensitivity that seems to arrive out of nowhere, are not problems at all? What if they are your body asking clearly and honestly for something it has not been given in a very long time?

Many of us learned early that our worth was connected to our output. We were praised for being strong, self-sufficient, reliable, endlessly available. So we became efficient and focused and always on. And underneath all of that is a nervous system in overdrive, bracing against the next thing, micromanaging, surviving. When the nervous system is in survival mode, rest does not feel safe. It actually feels like a threat. The body has learned to associate stillness with danger rather than recovery, so even when we finally sit down, some part of us does not actually stop.

Perimenopause and menopause can bring all of this into sharp focus because the things that used to work simply stop working. The drive that got you through everything before starts to feel like it is costing more than it is giving. And no one hands you a map for this. What I believe, and what I see in the women I practice with, is that this is not a breakdown. It is a pause that the body has been asking for, sometimes for years. Midlife is not the time to push harder. It is the time to soften, to listen, to return to the rhythm that is actually yours rather than the one you inherited or performed.

Slowness is not quitting. It is a different kind of intelligence, one that trusts the body to lead, that honors what you actually know rather than what you feel you should be able to do. To slow down is not to fall behind. It is to step fully into your own pace, and to discover what becomes possible when you stop overriding it.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
