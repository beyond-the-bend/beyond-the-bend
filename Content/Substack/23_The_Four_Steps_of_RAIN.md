# Finding a Map for Meditation

I've been asked so many times how to start meditating at home. And I completely get it. You know meditation would probably help, and you've likely thought about it or even tried it a few times. But then you sit down, close your eyes, and wonder what to do next. You wonder if you should just sit there, try to clear your mind, or count your breaths. Within about ninety seconds your brain is making tomorrow's grocery list or replaying a conversation from last week, and you feel like you are doing it wrong. The truth is that you aren't doing it wrong. You just don't have a map yet.

This week I want to share something that changed everything for me when I was learning to meditate. It's a practice called RAIN, developed by psychologist and meditation teacher Tara Brach. RAIN isn't about emptying your mind or achieving some blissed-out state. It is a gentle and structured way to actually be with yourself when you sit down to meditate. It gives you something to focus on, offering a way to meet whatever is present without trying to fix it, push it away, or pretend it isn't happening. Honestly, it is one of the most compassionate practices I know.

RAIN is an acronym that guides you through four simple and kind steps. Together, they create a complete meditation practice you can do in five minutes or fifty. The first step is to Recognize what is happening, which is simply noticing what is here right now. It sounds straightforward, but we skip this step constantly. We are so used to being in motion, reacting, fixing, and managing that we barely pause long enough to actually register what we are feeling. We just know something is off, and we are already trying to make it better. Recognizing is different because it means stopping, turning your attention inward, and naming what is actually present. Maybe it's anxiety sitting in your chest, or that familiar heaviness that shows up when you're overwhelmed. Maybe it's restlessness, sadness, irritation, or even just the relentless hum of a busy mind that won't settle. You don't need fancy language for this, and you aren't diagnosing yourself. You are simply acknowledging what is presently there.

There is something subtle but important to this step. There is a difference between saying you are anxious and saying you feel anxiety. One makes it who you are, while the other makes it something you are experiencing. When you identify with anger, you become it, but when you simply feel anger, there is space between you and the feeling. They are not the same thing. So when you recognize, you are naming what you feel rather than labeling who you are. You are witnessing an experience without defining yourself by it. The vital part is that you don't attach to it, try to fix it, or spin stories about why it is there. You just notice its presence. Sometimes this simple act of naming creates a tiny bit of space between you and the feeling. You are no longer completely fused with it, and that witnessing is where freedom starts.

The second step is to Allow the experience to be there, just as it is. This might be the hardest step because everything in us wants to fix, change, push away, or bypass what we don't like feeling. We have been conditioned to solve problems, to make things better, to not wallow, and to stay positive. When something uncomfortable arises, our first instinct is to shut it down, distract ourselves, or shame ourselves for feeling it in the first place. Allowing is the opposite of all of that. It is an internal decision acknowledging that a feeling can be there, without fighting it or pretending it isn't real. This doesn't mean you're saying it's okay, or that you want it to stay forever. It just means you aren't adding resistance on top of the pain or making it worse by going to war with yourself. You are opening the door and letting it sit for a while. What happens when you stop resisting is remarkable. The feeling doesn't necessarily go away, but the suffering around it often does. Because so much of our pain comes not from the feeling itself, but from our refusal to let it be, allowing creates space, softens the grip, and gives you room to breathe even when things are hard.

The third step is to Investigate with interest and care. This is where the practice can get tricky, so I want to be really clear about what this step is not. Investigation isn't analysis, and it isn't sitting there trying to figure out why you feel this way or where it came from. The moment your mind starts spinning, searching, and trying to solve, you have left your body and returned to your head. This kind of investigation is quieter and felt rather than thought. Tara is very clear about this in her teaching, noting that the gateway to healing is opening to your embodied experience, staying with the felt sense in your body instead of thinking about what's going on. You do this by asking gentle questions about what really wants your attention right now and where you feel it in your body. Bringing your attention to your throat, your chest, or your belly, you feel into the sensations to observe if they are tight, heavy, hot, or pulling. Then you just stay with it. You let it be there without needing to understand it or make it make sense. Sometimes when I sit with something long enough without trying to figure it out, the knowing comes on its own. It doesn't arrive as a thought, but rather as a softening or a realization in my body instead of my brain. If I go looking for answers with my intellect, starting that loop of what this means or where it came from, I lose it completely and fall back into the trance of thinking. Investigation here means feeling it securely, letting it speak to you in its own time, and trusting that your body knows things your mind hasn't figured out yet.

The final step is to Nurture with self-compassion. This is the step people rush through or skip entirely because it can feel so awkward. Someone tells you to put your hand on your heart and whisper something kind to yourself, and it can feel totally contrived, almost like playacting at self-love. But nurturing isn't about the gesture itself, or about saying the right words and putting your hand in the right place. It's about actually offering yourself something real. This step is about asking the vulnerable part of you what it needs right now, and then really listening with your whole body. Does this part of you need to hear that it isn't your fault? That you aren't broken, and that you are allowed to be tired, scared, or overwhelmed? Does it need to know it isn't alone, and that someone sees it, even if that someone is you? However it feels true for you, you offer that. It could be your hand on your heart, an exhale that softens your whole body, or just a quiet internal voice assuring yourself that you are there and that you are okay. What matters is that it's genuine, and that you are bringing it into your body to let yourself receive care, even if you're the one offering it. This isn't positive thinking or trying to convince yourself everything is fine when it isn't. It's meeting the truth of your suffering and acknowledging that even when things are hard, you deserve kindness anyway. Sometimes this step brings tears, sometimes relief, or sometimes just a tiny crack of light in a dark room. In the end, you're learning to be with yourself the way you'd be with someone you truly love, not fixing or rushing, but just being there.

I want you to know that every time you sit down to practice meditation, it doesn't have to be profound. It doesn't have to be a breakthrough or a big emotional release. Some days it will feel simple and subtle, and other days it might honestly feel like nothing at all. That is absolutely okay, and it's actually the practice itself. What we're really doing here is learning to be fully present with ourselves and to care about the person we're sitting with. That is something most of us never do. We continually distract ourselves and keep busy, putting ourselves in positions where we never have to look inward. We easily avoid the essence of who we are beneath all the noise and rarely take the time to acknowledge that we exist beneath all the doing, thinking, and proving. This practice isn't about fixing yourself or having an enlightenment moment every time you close your eyes. It is just a gentle welcoming, a quiet acknowledgment of who you really are, and a few minutes where you decide not to run from yourself. That is enough, and it is actually everything.

Starting a meditation practice doesn't require perfection. It just requires showing up, meeting yourself with kindness, and remembering that you're worth those few quiet minutes. I'm grateful to Tara Brach for developing this practice and sharing it so generously, and I hope RAIN gives you a way in. I hope it offers a structure gentle enough to hold you while you learn to hold yourself.
