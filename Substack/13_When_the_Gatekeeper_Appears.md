# When the Gatekeeper Appears

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been spending time with The Alchemist lately, a book I first read many years ago, but this time it is meeting me in a very different place. Instead of feeling inspirational or symbolic, it has been acting more like a mirror, drawing my attention to patterns I recognize in myself as I am living them right now.

One theme in particular has been staying with me: how fear shows up when something meaningful is beginning to shift. Not at the beginning of a journey, which is when I always expected it, but much later, when something already feels close. Real. When the shift has moved out of the realm of possibility and into the territory of actually happening. Once I saw that pattern in the book, I started seeing it in myself, and I have not been able to unsee it.

This kind of fear feels different from panic or ordinary uncertainty. It feels reasonable. Measured. It asks thoughtful questions, the kind that sound like self-protection rather than avoidance. Why now? Why disrupt what is working? Isn't this enough? At this stage of life, those questions carry real weight because they are not coming from inexperience. They are coming from knowing exactly what change costs, how much energy it takes to rebuild something, how hard-won a sense of peace can be. So the fear does not shout. It whispers. And it often sounds like wisdom.

In The Alchemist, fear appears not as something to conquer but more like a gatekeeper standing at a threshold. What has been striking me is realizing how often, in the past, I have mistaken that gatekeeper for a warning sign. I have taken its presence as evidence that I should not move forward, rather than as a signal that something meaningful was about to change. The gatekeeper tends to show up, as I understand it now, precisely when something inside has already begun to align. After the fantasizing has passed. After there is a quieter, steadier sense of yes, often felt in the body before it is clear in words. The fear does not erase that yes. It rises alongside it.

What has been shifting for me is learning not to run from that fear and not to immediately obey it either. Instead I am practicing staying with it, sitting in the discomfort, letting the fear be present without treating it as a verdict. Seeing it not as a message to abandon myself, but as a sign that something important is trying to come through. I do not have this mastered. I am very much living it. But it has changed how I meet those moments. The gatekeeper no longer feels like the end of the road. It feels like the place where something honest asks for courage, patience, and trust.

I cannot help but wonder how often this same fear appears for other women right at the edge of a real shift, not as a sign to stop, but as an invitation to stay long enough to see what is on the other side. That feels worth sitting with.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
