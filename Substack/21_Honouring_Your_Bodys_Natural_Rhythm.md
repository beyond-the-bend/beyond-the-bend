# Honouring Your Body's Natural Rhythm

*By Laura Harvey*

I came across a quote this week from Rumi that has been sitting with me since: try to accept the changing seasons of your heart, even as you have always accepted the passing seasons that move over your fields. The seasons of the earth unfold in their own time, without effort or argument from us. Fields grow, ripen, are harvested, and then rest, waiting in the quiet for what comes next. We accept these cycles as part of life, natural and inevitable. And yet we tend to resist the same movement in ourselves.

I have been learning something lately that I find genuinely fascinating, and I want to share it because it has been changing how I think about my own energy and the way I hold myself accountable to a kind of constant productivity that my body was simply never designed for. You may have heard of circadian rhythms, our 24-hour internal clock, the one that helps us wake with the light and feel sleepy as darkness comes, that keeps our hormones and temperature and digestion in a kind of natural balance. When we honor it, life feels smoother. When we fight it, as we so often do with late screens and early alarms and too much caffeine to compensate for what we missed, we feel it in ways that are hard to argue with.

But within that larger daily rhythm are smaller cycles, what scientists call ultradian rhythms, that last roughly 90 minutes each. For about an hour and a half we can be genuinely focused, alert, and engaged. Then, almost like clockwork, the body asks for a pause. That is the moment you notice yourself staring out the window, craving something, losing the thread of whatever you were doing. Most of us push through that signal, maybe with coffee or willpower or the sheer momentum of a full schedule. But the body was actually asking for a few minutes to reset, so it could begin the next cycle genuinely refreshed rather than gradually depleted.

To me this feels like such a kind design. The body is not asking us to go nonstop. It is built to have seasons within the day, small periods of focus and small periods of rest, and the two are meant to alternate rather than compete. We have been taught that productivity is continuous effort, that rest is earned only after the work is done, that slowing down is a luxury or a failure of discipline. But that is not how any of this actually works. It is not how the earth works, and it is clearly not how we work either.

The invitation in this is simpler than it might sound. It is not about restructuring your entire day or finding perfect conditions for rest. It is about noticing the natural dip when it comes, trusting it rather than overriding it, and offering yourself even two minutes of actual stopping before pushing on. That small pause is not indulgence. It is how the body naturally comes back into balance.

Just like the outer seasons teach us to honor change, our inner rhythms are asking us to honor rest.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
