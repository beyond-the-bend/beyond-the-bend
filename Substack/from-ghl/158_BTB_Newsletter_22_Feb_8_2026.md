## Hello

I have been spending some time lately watching the way the light is beginning to shift here in Edmonton. Even in the depths of winter, there is a subtle movement toward something new, if we are quiet enough to notice it.

This sense of ongoing movement is what inspired today’s reflection. For as long as I can remember, I have looked at life as a continuous adventure. I have always assumed everyone felt this way - that we are meant to keep expanding and discovering new layers of ourselves as the years go by.

But lately, in conversations, I have realized that isn't a universal truth. Some have been told that growth is for the young and that eventually we are just supposed to be finished. This week, I want to share a perspective that offers a much more hopeful roadmap for women in our prime.

✨ My invitation for you this month: Notice where you’re choosing “busy” over “peace,” and gently ask yourself — is this stress actually helping me?

September arrives with its own kind of hum. For many of us, it stirs up the old rhythm of back-to-school, fresh starts, new schedules — and with it, a rush of shoulds and self-imposed pressure.

Suddenly our minds are full of lists: things to organize, tasks to complete, details that must get handled. And if we don’t tick every box, the inner critic shows up: “Why can’t I keep up?”

But here’s the thing: stress doesn’t make the work go faster. Busyness doesn’t always mean productivity. Often, it’s just noise.

What if September could feel different this year?

What if, instead of being pulled into the whirlwind, you brought a little spaciousness into your days? A pause between the tasks. A few deep breaths before the next thing. A moment to remember: peace isn’t earned after the list is done — it’s available right here, in the middle of it all.

Because when the mind is calmer, the noise fades. And when the noise fades, what truly matters has room to rise.

## Holding the Pen

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga


I find the work of psychologist Robert Kegan very helpful because he gives a concrete foundation to that sense of adventure I have always felt in my bones. He suggests that as we move into our prime years, we often go through a specific shift in how we see ourselves.

For much of our lives, many of us have been like actors reading from a script written by everyone else. We spend decades perfecting the roles of the helpful daughter, the tireless mother, or the reliable employee. We are so focused on meeting the expectations of the room that we can actually lose the sound of our own voice. We aren't doing it wrong; we are simply being "the good student" the world told us to be.

But then, a quiet widening begins to happen.

We start to realize that while we still have those roles, they are not actually who we are. We begin to step back from the script and notice that we are the ones holding the pen. This is the moment we start to decide for ourselves how we want to show up, what we truly value, and where we want to go next. We move from following the story to finally writing it.

In the practice room, I see this as a new kind of freedom. It shows up when we find just a little more space between a thought and a reaction. We start to loosen our grip on the old "shoulds" that no longer fit as comfortably as they once did. We stop trying to "fix" ourselves and start to simply listen.

Realizing we are not finished is a privilege. It means we are alive to our lives. If you have been sensing a new clarity or a softening within yourself, recognize it for what it is. This isn't restlessness. It is development.

A human life is not meant to close in on itself. It is meant to keep opening. We still get to grow, not with the urgency of our earlier years, but with a steadiness that allows what is emerging to unfold at its own pace.

We are, all of us, still becoming.

Imagine, for a moment, our inner world as a garden. Every thought, every act of care, every boundary and breath becomes part of its landscape. Some days it may look lush and alive — new growth reaching toward the sun. Other days, it may feel untended — overgrown with old habits or dry from neglect.

This garden isn’t a place to perfect. It’s a place to notice.
To be curious about what’s growing.
To acknowledge what’s struggling.
To gently tend what needs attention.

When we nurture ourselves with gentleness and honesty, that energy naturally extends outward. It softens our tone, steadies our presence, and deepens our capacity to love others without losing ourselves. But when we neglect our inner garden — through self-criticism, avoidance, or over-giving — the soil becomes depleted. Even when love comes near, it can’t quite take root.

Many of us learned long ago to earn love through doing, pleasing, or achieving. Those patterns were intelligent; they kept us connected in the ways we knew how. But as we grow older, they begin to feel heavy — as if the garden we’ve been tending was built to please everyone but ourselves.

Healing begins the moment we bring awareness to this cycle — when we pause, take a breath, and ask, What would it feel like to meet ourselves with curiosity instead of judgment?

That single question is like sunlight breaking through clouds. It doesn’t demand that we fix or change — only that we notice, and allow. Each act of awareness becomes a drop of water for the roots of self-trust. Each kind thought becomes nourishment for the soil of our hearts.

Over time, the garden shifts. We begin to see new shoots of growth — boundaries formed from clarity rather than fear, choices made from love rather than guilt, tenderness that doesn’t depend on anyone else’s approval.

We don’t need to be “fixed” or endlessly giving to be worthy of love. We only need to meet ourselves where we are — with patience, tenderness, and care.

Because when we do, our roots grow stronger. And from that fertile ground, every connection in our lives — with others, with the world, and with our own hearts — begins to bloom in a way that feels more peaceful, more authentic, and more true.

# Your Sanctuary, Right Where You Are

If our conversation about "holding the pen" resonated with you, I want to offer a very concrete way to practice that choice. For many of us, the act of getting to a yoga studio can feel like just another script we are trying to follow. There is the rush to get out the door, the traffic, the parking, and the pressure of being on time for the world. Sometimes, the most self-authored thing we can do is decide to stay exactly where we are.

When you join us online, you are not just watching a pre-recorded video. You are joining a real circle of women gathering at Rising Moon Studio in real time. You get to borrow the energy of the room, the warmth of the fireplace, and the steady pace of the group, all while remaining in the sanctuary of your own home. It removes the hustle. You can move, breathe, and settle into your body in a way that fits your real life, exactly as it is today.

This online space offers a soft landing. It allows you to show up comfortably, without expectations or the need to perform for anyone else. If you can click a link in your email, you can do this. You do not need fancy equipment or a special setup, just you and your mat.

## Practice of the Week

**The Author’s Pause**

This week, I invite you to notice the moments when you feel the "pull" of an old script. This isn't about judging yourself; it is just about noticing.

- **The Script Check:** Several times today, simply pause and ask, *"Who wrote the script for what I am doing right now?"*


- **The Sensation:** Notice if your shoulders are up or if your breath is shallow. Often, when we are reading someone else's script, our bodies feel a bit tight or rushed.


- **The Hand-over-Heart Reset:** Place one hand on your heart and take three slow breaths. As you exhale, imagine you are putting the script down on a table.


- **The Pen:** Ask yourself, *"If I were holding the pen right now, what is the very next thing I would choose to do?"* It might be as simple as getting a glass of water, taking a longer exhale, or saying no to a request that feels heavy.


Let this be enough. Each time you pause, you are practicing the art of becoming the author of your own day.
