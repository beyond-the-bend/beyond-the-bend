# The Turning World, The Still Point, and You

*By Laura Harvey*

I have been sitting with a specific image all week, one that feels like it belongs to this time of year in a way I cannot quite shake. It comes from the poet T.S. Eliot, who wrote about what he called the still point of the turning world. He was trying to describe the place that exists at the center of all movement, the part that does not spin even though everything around it does.

I keep returning to this because it describes something I recognize in my own life. It feels like living on the edge of a giant, spinning wheel. There is the to-do list, the caretaking, the business of living, the schedules, the noise. That outer edge spins incredibly fast, and when I am living entirely out there, I get dizzy and scattered. I catch myself thinking that if I just spin a little faster, or manage the systems a little better, I will finally catch up and find some peace. Of course the spinning never actually stops. The wheel keeps turning because the wheel has to turn. The laundry needs doing. The work needs tending. None of that is wrong.

But look at a wheel for a moment. The hub at the center, the axle, carries all the weight. It is what allows all the movement to happen. And the hub itself does not spin. Right there at the center of all that turning, there is something completely still.

That is what I am interested in. Not stopping the wheel, because I cannot and I do not need to, but moving my seat. Shifting attention from the exhausted, dizzying rim back toward the hub. When I practice, whether in movement or in stillness, I am not trying to fix the chaos or become more efficient at spinning. I am simply remembering that there is a place inside, in the breath, in the spine, in whatever I would call my deeper self, that is the still point. It is the part that moves through life but does not spin out.

This is not a one-time arrival. It is a practice of returning, again and again, and some days the return is easier than others. Some days I find myself flung back out to the edge, caught up in urgency or overwhelm or the hundred small emergencies of daily life. That is okay. The hub is always there, waiting. The body knows how to find it. The breath knows the way back.

For some women the still point shows up in a long-held restorative pose, when the nervous system finally exhales and the body seems to say oh, I am safe. For others it is the moment in meditation when the mental chatter softens and they realize they have been watching their thoughts instead of becoming them. And sometimes it is as plain as noticing your feet on the ground and remembering: I am here. I am whole. I do not have to spin.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
