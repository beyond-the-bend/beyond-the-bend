# The Garden Within

*By Laura Harvey*

There is a quote I have been sitting with this week, from the teacher Matt Kahn: you can only meet someone as deeply as you have met yourself. The first time I read it, something in me went still. Because I think I knew instinctively that it was true, and I also knew how much of my life I had spent in relationships of all kinds, with partners, with friends, with family, while still being somewhat of a stranger to myself.

Our connections are mirrors. Tender, revealing, sometimes uncomfortable ones. They reflect how we speak to ourselves, how safe we feel in our own company, how much compassion we are willing to extend inward. And when we begin to see that clearly, it changes how we move through love. Not as something to find or earn or chase, but as something that begins, quite literally, within. The way we treat ourselves becomes the soil from which every other relationship grows.

I have been imagining the inner world as a garden. Every thought, every act of care, every boundary set or swallowed, every breath taken intentionally or forgotten entirely, all of it becomes part of the landscape. Some days it looks lush and alive, new growth reaching toward the sun, things rooting deeply. Other days it feels untended, dry from neglect, overgrown with old habits that I keep meaning to address. This garden is not a place to perfect. It is a place to notice, to be curious about what is growing, to acknowledge what is struggling, to gently tend what needs attention without judgment about how it got there.

When we nurture ourselves with honesty and gentleness, that energy extends outward naturally. It softens our tone in difficult conversations. It steadies our presence when someone we love is going through something hard. It deepens our capacity to be with people without losing ourselves in the process. But when we neglect the inner garden, through self-criticism, avoidance, or years of giving more than we receive, the soil becomes depleted. Even when love comes near, it struggles to take root.

Many of us learned long ago to earn love through doing and pleasing and achieving. Those patterns were intelligent responses to what we needed at the time. They kept us connected in the ways available to us. But as we grow older, they begin to feel heavy, as though the garden we have been tending was built to please everyone but ourselves. Healing begins the moment we bring awareness to that cycle, when we pause and ask what it would feel like to meet ourselves with curiosity instead of judgment. That single question is like sunlight breaking through clouds. It does not demand that we fix anything, only that we notice, and allow.

We do not need to be further along than we are to be worthy of tending to our own ground. We only need to meet ourselves where we actually are, with patience and care. Because from that kind of tending, the connections in our lives, with others, with the world, with our own hearts, begin to grow in a way that feels more honest, more sustainable, and more fully ours.

With warmth and gratitude,
Laura
