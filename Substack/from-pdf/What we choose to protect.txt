Stillness, Commerce, and What We Choose to
Protect

By Laura Harvey | Beyond the Bend Yoga

As I've been developing Beyond the Bend Yoga, I've been grappling
with something I want to share honestly with you.

I care deeply about offering something that genuinely supports people.
Something that helps them feel steadier in themselves, less rushed,
more at home in their own bodies. And at the same time, I'm building a
business. There are websites and schedules and pricing and systems.
None of that is inherently wrong. It's just the landscape we're working in.

But here's the thing. Yoga didn't begin there. It didn't start as an industry
or a strategy or something to package and promote. It began as a
personal relationship with breath, sensation, and awareness. And I
notice how easily the center of gravity can shift. When a practice rooted
in listening becomes something we consume, our attention often moves
outward. Am I doing this right? Is this what it's supposed to look like?
Am I keeping up? Without meaning to, yoga can become another place
where we strive, compare, or shape ourselves rather than soften into
what's actually happening.

I've felt this tension in my own body. The pull to keep up. To offer more.
To make things look a certain way. To fit into the language of productivity
and progress. It's not really a conflict so much as a constant balancing
act, a remembering again and again of why I'm here in the first place.

Yoga was never meant to make us more efficient or impressive. It was
meant to help us become more present. To notice. To feel. To respond
rather than react.

And look, money isn't the issue. Teachers deserve to be paid. Spaces
need to be supported. Many people are offering their work with real care
and integrity. At the same time, it's worth noticing how easily depth can
get overshadowed when there's pressure to produce or perform.
Subtlety doesn't always translate well online. Stillness doesn't announce
itself.

What feels most important to me is paying attention to how
commercialization can subtly undermine inner authority. When students
are taught, even unintentionally, to look outside themselves for
validation, correction, or approval, they can lose trust in their own
experience. A practice meant to cultivate agency can, without care, do
the opposite.

I don't have tidy answers to any of this. What I do have is an ongoing
awareness of the tension I'm holding as I develop this business. I want
to offer something that truly supports people in slowing down, listening
inward, and reconnecting with a sense of stillness that already exists
inside them. And I'm doing that within systems that can easily pull us in
the opposite direction.

I see how quickly yoga can lose its center when it becomes something
to sell, scale, or perfect. How easily it can turn into another place where
we perform, strive, or look outside ourselves for cues about what's right.
That loss of connection to the deeper purpose of the practice is exactly
what I'm trying to stay awake to, both for myself and for the people who
practice with me.

So this work, for me, is a continual act of remembering. Remembering
why I began. Remembering what yoga has offered me when it's been at
its most honest. And remembering that stillness isn't something I can
package or promise. It's something I can protect space for.

That commitment shapes how I teach, how I structure what I offer, and
how I move through the business side of things. Not perfectly. Not
without friction. But with care.

Because the last thing I want is for yoga to become another place where

OUR WEEKLY PRACTICE:

Self-Inquiry
This week, I'm inviting you to sit with a few questions. Not to answer
them perfectly or figure anything out. Just to notice what comes up
when you ask.

Find a quiet moment. Maybe with your journal, maybe just with
yourself. And gently ask:

Where am I performing instead of being present?

What happens in my body when I look outside myself for permission or
validation?

What am I protecting space for in my life right now?

Where do I already know what's true, even if I haven't acted on it yet?

You don't need to answer all of them. You don't need to answer any of
them completely. Just let them sit with you. Notice what surfaces.
Notice what your body knows before your mind tries to organize it into
something neat.

That's the practice. Just noticing. Just listening.
